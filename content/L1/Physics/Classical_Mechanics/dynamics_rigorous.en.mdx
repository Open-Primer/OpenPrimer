# 1. The Vectorial Nature of Force and Momentum

In classical Newtonian mechanics, the motion of a particle is governed by its interactions with its environment, quantified through the concept of **Force** ($\vec{F}$). To understand dynamics at a university level, we must move beyond scalar approximations and embrace the full vectorial and differential nature of these laws.

### 1.1 Linear Momentum and the Generalized Second Law
The state of motion of a particle is defined by its **linear momentum** $\vec{p}$, which is the product of its mass $m$ and its velocity $\vec{v}$:
$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$

Newton's Second Law, in its most rigorous form, states that the net force acting on a particle is equal to the **time rate of change** of its linear momentum:
$$\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \frac{d(m\vec{v})}{dt}$$

If the mass $m$ remains constant (which is true for most non-relativistic systems at L1 level), the equation simplifies to the familiar:
$$\vec{F} = m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = m\vec{a}$$

# 2. Differential Equations of Motion

The Second Law is essentially a **second-order ordinary differential equation (ODE)**. Solving for the trajectory $\vec{r}(t)$ requires integrating the acceleration:
$$m\frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2} = \vec{F}(\vec{r}, \dot{\vec{r}}, t)$$

### 2.1 Example: The Linear Harmonic Oscillator
Consider a mass $m$ attached to a spring with stiffness constant $k$. The restoring force is given by Hooke's Law: $F = -kx$. The ODE becomes:
$$m\ddot{x} + kx = 0 \implies \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0$$
where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the natural angular frequency.

The general solution is a linear combination of sinusoids:
$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi)$$

<SolvedProblem>
**Problem: Damped Oscillations in a Viscous Fluid**
A particle of mass $m$ is subject to a restoring force $-kx$ and a drag force proportional to its velocity $F_d = -b\dot{x}$. 

**1. Derive the ODE:**
$$m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0$$

**2. Solve for the Underdamped Case ($b < 2\sqrt{mk}$):**
We assume a solution of the form $x(t) = e^{\lambda t}$. Substituting into the ODE gives the characteristic equation:
$$m\lambda^2 + b\lambda + k = 0 \implies \lambda = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4mk}}{2m}$$
Defining $\gamma = b/2m$ and $\omega = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}$, the solution is:
$$x(t) = A e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$

**Conclusion:** The amplitude decays exponentially over time due to the non-conservative drag force.
</SolvedProblem>

# 3. Work, Energy, and Potential Gradients

A force is **conservative** if the work done by it on a particle moving between two points is independent of the path taken. In such cases, we can define a **Potential Energy** $U(\vec{r})$.

### 3.1 The Gradient Relationship
A conservative force is the negative gradient of its potential energy:
$$\vec{F} = -\nabla U = - \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial U}{\partial z}\hat{k} \right)$$

For a gravitational potential $U(z) = mgz$:
$$\vec{F} = -\frac{\partial (mgz)}{\partial z}\hat{k} = -mg\hat{k}$$

<ActiveRecall>
**Question:** Why does a non-conservative force (like friction) not have a potential energy function?
**Answer:** Because the work done depends on the path length. The line integral $\oint \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} \neq 0$, meaning the curl $\nabla \times \vec{F} \neq 0$.
</ActiveRecall>

# 4. Systems with Variable Mass (The Rocket Equation)

In systems where mass is being ejected (like a rocket), we must return to the original form of Newton's Second Law: $\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$.

Let $v_e$ be the velocity of the ejected mass relative to the rocket. The thrust force is:
$$F_{thrust} = v_e \frac{dm}{dt}$$

The final velocity of a rocket starting from rest is:
$$v_f = v_e \ln\left(\frac{m_{initial}}{m_{final}}\right)$$
This is the **Tsiolkovsky rocket equation**, a cornerstone of aerospace dynamics.
