# 1. Axiomatic Foundations of Classical Dynamics

Newtonian mechanics is built upon three fundamental axioms that describe the relationship between the motion of a body and the forces acting upon it. At the university level (L1), we must move beyond the basic definitions and explore the differential and vectorial consequences of these laws.

### 1.1 The Concept of the Inertial Frame
Newton's laws are only valid in a specific class of reference frames called **Inertial Reference Frames** (or Galilean frames). An inertial frame is one in which a particle not subject to any net force moves with constant velocity.

The transition between two inertial frames $R$ and $R'$ moving at constant velocity $\vec{V}$ is given by the **Galilean Transformation**:
$$\vec{r}' = \vec{r} - \vec{V}t$$
$$\vec{v}' = \vec{v} - \vec{V}$$
$$\vec{a}' = \vec{a}$$

Since acceleration is invariant under Galilean transformations, the laws of dynamics remain the same in all inertial frames.

# 2. Newton's Second Law: The Differential Perspective

The most critical equation in classical mechanics relates the net force $\sum \vec{F}$ to the rate of change of linear momentum $\vec{p}$:
$$\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

For a particle of constant mass $m$, this becomes the second-order ordinary differential equation (ODE):
$$m\frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2} = \sum \vec{F}_{ext}(\vec{r}, \dot{\vec{r}}, t)$$

### 2.1 Projectile Motion with Viscous Drag
Consider a particle under gravity and linear air resistance $\vec{F}_d = -b\vec{v}$. The equation of motion is:
$$m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = m\vec{g} - b\vec{v}$$

This is a first-order linear differential equation for $\vec{v}(t)$. Separating variables for the vertical component:
$$m\frac{dv_z}{dt} = -mg - bv_z \implies \frac{dv_z}{v_z + mg/b} = -\frac{b}{m}dt$$

Integrating gives the terminal velocity $v_T = -mg/b$:
$$v_z(t) = (v_{z0} - v_T)e^{-bt/m} + v_T$$

# 3. Systems with Variable Mass: The Tsiolkovsky Equation

In cases where mass $m(t)$ changes due to ejection or accretion, we must use the general form:
$$\vec{F}_{ext} + \vec{v}_{rel}\frac{dm}{dt} = m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$$
where $\vec{v}_{rel}$ is the velocity of the ejected mass relative to the system.

For a rocket in vacuum ($\vec{F}_{ext} = 0$):
$$-v_e \frac{dm}{dt} = m \frac{dv}{dt} \implies dv = -v_e \frac{dm}{m}$$

Integrating from $m_i$ to $m_f$:
$$\Delta v = v_e \ln\left(\frac{m_i}{m_f}\right)$$
