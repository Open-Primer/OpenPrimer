# Introduction to Structural Biochemistry: The Chemistry of Life

Welcome to the **Structural Biochemistry** L1 course. This course addresses the chemistry of life's fundamental molecules — proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids — focusing on the structure-function relationship that governs all of molecular biology.

<SelfEval title="Introduction to Structural Biochemistry" type="pre" />

## 1. Why Structural Biochemistry?

Structural biochemistry answers a fundamental question: **how does a molecule's structure determine its function?** This relationship is at the heart of all therapeutics (drug design), all biotechnology (industrial enzymes), and all understanding of molecular diseases.

## 2. The Four Classes of Macromolecules

| Macromolecule | Monomer | Main Functions |
|--------------|---------|---------------|
| **Proteins** | Amino acids | Catalysis, structure, signaling, immunity |
| **Carbohydrates** | Monosaccharides | Energy, structure (cellulose, chitin), signaling |
| **Lipids** | Fatty acids, glycerol | Membranes, energy storage, hormonal signaling |
| **Nucleic acids** | Nucleotides | Genetic information, catalysis (ribozymes) |

## 3. Course Structure

This course will cover:

- **Module 1 — Proteins**: Primary structure (sequence), secondary (α-helix, β-sheet), tertiary (3D folding), and quaternary. Enzymology: Michaelis-Menten kinetics, inhibition, allosteric regulation.

- **Module 2 — Carbohydrates and Lipids**: Sugar structure (aldoses/ketoses, anomers, enantiomers), structural and storage polysaccharides. Saturated/unsaturated fatty acids, triglycerides, phospholipids, and glycolipids.

- **Module 3 — Vitamins and Coenzymes**: Role of coenzymes (NAD⁺, FAD, CoA, TPP) in metabolism; water-soluble and fat-soluble vitamins.

## 4. Essential Prerequisites

<MetaNote title="Fundamental Chemical Bonds">
  Master **covalent** bonds (strong, ~350 kJ/mol), **ionic**, **hydrogen** bonds (~20 kJ/mol), **Van der Waals** (~1-4 kJ/mol), and **hydrophobic** interactions. Macromolecular structures result from the sum of thousands of these weak interactions.
</MetaNote>
