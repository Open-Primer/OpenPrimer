# Introduction to Molecular Genetics: From Gene to Protein

Welcome to the **Molecular Genetics** L1 course. This course explores the fundamental mechanism by which hereditary information encoded in DNA is read, copied, and translated into functional proteins — the very heart of modern biology.

<SelfEval title="Introduction to Molecular Genetics" type="pre" />

## 1. The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

Molecular genetics rests on a founding principle stated by Francis Crick in 1958: **the Central Dogma**. It describes the unidirectional flow of genetic information:

> **DNA → RNA → Protein**

- **Replication**: DNA copies itself (transmission to daughter cells)
- **Transcription**: DNA is transcribed into messenger RNA (mRNA)
- **Translation**: mRNA is translated into an amino acid sequence (protein)

<MetaNote title="Exceptions to the Central Dogma">
  The discovery of **reverse transcriptase** (Howard Temin & David Baltimore, Nobel Prize 1975) showed that certain retroviruses (like HIV) can transcribe RNA into DNA — a reverse flow. The central dogma describes the standard flow, but exceptions exist.
</MetaNote>

## 2. Course Structure

This course is organized into two fundamental modules:

- **Module 1 — DNA Replication and Structure**: You will learn the double helix structure of DNA, its organization into chromosomes, and the precise mechanisms of semi-conservative replication.

- **Module 2 — Gene Expression**: Transcription in prokaryotes and eukaryotes, mRNA maturation, and translation mechanisms at the ribosome level.

## 3. Why Molecular Genetics?

Understanding molecular genetics is the key to understanding:
- **Genetic diseases** (cancer, hereditary diseases)
- **Modern biotechnologies** (CRISPR-Cas9, NGS sequencing)
- **Evolution** at the molecular level (phylogenomics)
- **Pharmacology** (drug targets, resistances)
