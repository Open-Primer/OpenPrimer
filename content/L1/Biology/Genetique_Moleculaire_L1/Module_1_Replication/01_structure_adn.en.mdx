# Lesson 1: DNA Structure and Chromosomal Organization

DNA (DeoxyriboNucleic Acid) is the molecule that carries all hereditary information of all living organisms. Its structure, revealed by Watson, Crick, Franklin, and Wilkins in 1953, is one of the most revolutionary discoveries in the history of science.

<SelfEval title="DNA Structure and Chromosomal Organization" type="pre" />

## 1. The Double Helix: Molecular Anatomy

### 1.1 Nucleotides: The Building Blocks

A nucleotide is composed of three elements:
1. A **pentose sugar** (deoxyribose for DNA, ribose for RNA)
2. A **phosphate group** (negatively charged, makes DNA acidic)
3. One of four **nitrogenous bases**:
   - **Purines** (bicyclic): Adenine (A), Guanine (G)
   - **Pyrimidines** (monocyclic): Cytosine (C), Thymine (T) — replaced by Uracil (U) in RNA

### 1.2 The Sugar-Phosphate Backbone

Nucleotides are connected by **phosphodiester bonds** between carbon 3' of one nucleotide's sugar and carbon 5' of the next. The DNA strand therefore has a **5'→3' polarity** — it is always read and synthesized in this direction.

<MetaNote title="Chargaff's Rules (1950)">
  Erwin Chargaff discovered that in any double-stranded DNA: **%A = %T** and **%G = %C**. This rule, unexplained at the time, would find its explanation in the base complementarity of the double helix.
</MetaNote>

### 1.3 The Double Helix: Complementarity and Antiparallel Orientation

In the DNA double helix:
- The two strands are **antiparallel**: one is oriented 5'→3', the other 3'→5'
- Bases are paired by **hydrogen bonds** according to the complementarity rule:
  - **A = T**: 2 hydrogen bonds
  - **G ≡ C**: 3 hydrogen bonds (more stable)

> The G≡C pair, being more stable, is more represented in DNA regions that must withstand high temperatures (thermophilic DNA) or require particular structural stability (promoter regions).

**Geometric parameters of B-helix (dominant physiological form):**
- Pitch: **3.4 nm** (10 base pairs per turn)
- Diameter: **2 nm**
- Two unequal grooves: **major groove** (12 Å) and **minor groove** (6 Å)

## 2. Chromosomal Organization: From Bare DNA to Condensed Chromosome

The human genome contains approximately **3.2 billion base pairs** of DNA per haploid nucleus, a total length of approximately **2 meters** if one were to unfold all the strands of a single cell. This packaging challenge is solved through several levels of organization.

### 2.1 Chromatin: Fundamental Structure

Chromatin is the DNA-protein complex present in the nucleus. The proteins associated with DNA are mainly **histones** (rich in lysine and arginine).

### 2.2 The Nucleosome: First Level of Packaging

The **nucleosome** is the basic unit of chromatin:
- **Core**: histone octamer (2× H2A, 2× H2B, 2× H3, 2× H4)
- **Wrapped DNA**: ~147 bp of DNA wraps 1.75 times around the octamer
- **Linker DNA**: ~20-60 bp of DNA connects nucleosomes, associated with histone **H1**

> Visualized by electron microscopy, the nucleosome filament resembles a **"beads-on-a-string"** pattern. This first level of compaction condenses DNA by a factor of ~7.

### 2.3 Higher Levels of Compaction

| Level | Structure | Compaction Factor |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Bare DNA | — | 1× |
| Nucleosomes (beads on string) | 11 nm | ~7× |
| 30 nm fiber (solenoid) | 6 nucleosomes/turn | ~40× |
| 300 nm loops | Anchored to nuclear matrix | ~200× |
| Condensed metaphase chromosome | — | ~10,000× |

### 2.4 Euchromatin vs. Heterochromatin

- **Euchromatin**: decondensed chromatin, transcriptionally active, gene-rich
- **Heterochromatin**: condensed chromatin, silent, contains repetitive sequences

<MetaNote title="Histone Modifications: The Epigenetic Code">
  Histones undergo **post-translational modifications**: acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation, ubiquitylation... These modifications alter chromatin compaction and regulate gene expression without changing the DNA sequence — this is **epigenetics**.
</MetaNote>

## 3. Functional Elements of Eukaryotic Chromosomes

A functional eukaryotic chromosome requires three essential elements:

1. **Replication origins (ARS)**: sequences recognized by pre-replication complexes (ORC)
2. **Telomeres**: tandem repeat sequences (TTAGGG in vertebrates) that protect chromosome ends from degradation and fusion
3. **Centromere**: region for kinetochore attachment and mitotic spindle microtubules

> **Telomeres** shorten with each cell division (end-replication problem). **Telomerase** (ribonucleoprotein) elongates telomeres in stem cells, germinal cells, and cancer cells — it is a major focus of cancer and aging research.
