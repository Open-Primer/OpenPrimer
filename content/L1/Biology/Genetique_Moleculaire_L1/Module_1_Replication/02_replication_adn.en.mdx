# Lesson 2: DNA Replication — Mechanisms and Fidelity

DNA replication is the process by which a cell copies its genome before dividing, ensuring that each daughter cell receives a faithful and complete copy of the genetic information. Its precision is staggering: one error per ~10¹⁰ integrated nucleotides.

<SelfEval title="DNA Replication" type="pre" />

## 1. Principle: Semi-Conservative Replication

The Meselson and Stahl experiment (1958), using heavy isotope ¹⁵N to label parental DNA, demonstrated that replication is **semi-conservative**: each daughter DNA molecule consists of one **parental strand** (conserved) and one **newly synthesized strand**.

This model was opposed to the two rejected alternatives:
- **Conservative**: parental DNA is preserved intact, and an entirely new double helix is synthesized
- **Dispersive**: parental strands are fragmented and randomly distributed

## 2. Steps of Replication

### 2.1 Initiation: Opening the Fork

Replication begins at specific DNA sequences called **replication origins** (oriC in *E. coli*, multiple ARS in eukaryotes).

**In Eukaryotes:**
1. The **ORC (Origin Recognition Complex)** binds to ARS throughout the cell cycle
2. In G1, **Cdc6** and **Cdt1** recruit **MCM2-7** helicases (hexamers) → formation of the **pre-replication complex (pre-RC)**
3. At S-phase entry, **CDK-S** and **DDK** kinases phosphorylate and activate MCMs → replication starts

### 2.2 Fork Progression

At the **replication fork**, several enzymes act in concert:

| Enzyme | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Helicase (MCM2-7)** | Unwinds the double helix (consumes ATP) |
| **SSB / RPA** | Stabilizes separated strands (single-strand binding proteins) |
| **Topoisomerase I** | Resolves positive supercoils ahead of the fork |
| **Topoisomerase II** | Decatenates daughter chromosomes |
| **Primase** | Synthesizes short RNA primers ~10 nt |
| **DNA Polymerase δ/ε** | Synthesizes leading strand (ε) and lagging strand (δ) |
| **RNase H / FEN1** | Removes RNA primers |
| **DNA Ligase** | Joins Okazaki fragments |

### 2.3 The Polarity Problem

**DNA polymerase** can only synthesize DNA **5'→3'**. Yet the two template strands are antiparallel. Solution:

- **Leading strand**: continuous synthesis 5'→3', in the direction of fork progression
- **Lagging strand**: discontinuous synthesis **5'→3'** but **opposite to fork direction** → production of **Okazaki fragments** (~100-200 nt in eukaryotes, ~1000-2000 nt in prokaryotes), each preceded by an RNA primer

<MetaNote title="Okazaki Fragments">
  Reiji Okazaki (1968) discovered these short fragments by incorporating radioactive thymidine for very short periods. The discontinuous synthesis of the lagging strand involves looping the template — the **replisome** keeps both polymerases associated, forming a loop in the lagging strand template.
</MetaNote>

## 3. Molecular Actors in Replication Fidelity

The precision of replication (~10⁻¹⁰ errors per base) relies on three successive mechanisms:

### 3.1 Intrinsic Selectivity of DNA Polymerase

DNA Pol δ/ε tests the geometry of each base pair in its **active site** before catalysis. A mismatch alters the site geometry → spontaneous error rate: **~10⁻⁵** per base.

### 3.2 3'→5' Exonuclease Activity (Proofreading)

DNA Pol δ/ε has an integrated **3'→5' exonuclease** activity: if an incorrect base is incorporated, the polymerase "backs up," excises the wrong nucleotide, and reinserts the correct one. Improvement: **~10⁻² to 10⁻³** → final error rate: **~10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁸** per base.

### 3.3 Post-Replication Mismatch Repair (MMR)

The **MMR system** detects and corrects residual mismatches after replication (MutS/MutL in prokaryotes; MSH2-MSH6/MLH1 in eukaryotes). Final error rate: **~10⁻¹⁰** per base.

## 4. Replication Cycle Control

Replication must be **triggered only once per cell cycle**, at each origin. Several mechanisms contribute:

- **Destruction of Cdc6 and Cdt1** after initiation (prevents pre-RC reassembly)
- **ORC phosphorylation** by CDKs (inactivates ORC after initiation)
- **Geminin** inhibits Cdt1 outside of G1

> Cancer is often characterized by dysregulation of cell cycle control: loss of S/G2M checkpoints, cyclin overexpression, Rb or p53 mutations.
