# Lesson 2: Cellular Organelles — Compartments and Functions

The eukaryotic cell is a compartmentalized system of extraordinary complexity. Its organelles are not simple enzyme-filled sacs: they are ultra-specialized functional units, coordinated in time and space, collectively carrying out the thousands of biochemical reactions necessary for life.

<SelfEval title="Cellular Organelles" type="pre" />

## 1. The Nucleus: The Genetic Command Center

The **nucleus** is the largest organelle (~5-10 μm in diameter). It contains the cell's complete genome and constitutes the site of DNA replication and transcription.

### 1.1 Nuclear Envelope

The nuclear envelope consists of **two concentric membranes** (outer and inner nuclear membranes) pierced by **nuclear pores** — protein complexes (~120 nm) that regulate bidirectional exchanges:
- **Nuclear import**: histones, transcription factors, RNA polymerases
- **Nuclear export**: mature mRNAs, rRNAs, ribosomal subunits

### 1.2 The Nucleolus

The **nucleolus** is a non-membranous nuclear sub-compartment, visible by light microscopy. It is the site of **rRNA synthesis and maturation** and pre-ribosomal subunit assembly. It disappears during mitosis and reforms in G1 phase.

<MetaNote title="Nuclear Lamina">
  The inner nuclear envelope is lined with the **nuclear lamina** — a network of intermediate filaments (lamins A, B, C) that confers rigidity to the nucleus and participates in chromatin organization. Mutations in lamin genes cause **laminopathies** (including progeria).
</MetaNote>

## 2. The Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The endoplasmic reticulum is a membrane network continuous with the outer nuclear envelope. It represents more than **50% of the cell's internal membranes**.

### 2.1 Rough ER (RER)

Studded with **ribosomes** on its cytosolic face, the RER is the site of synthesis for proteins destined for:
- **Secretion** (insulin, antibodies, collagen)
- **Membranes** (integral proteins)
- The **endomembrane system** (lysosomes, Golgi apparatus)

Protein synthesis at the RER involves **co-translational translocation**: the nascent protein is transferred into the ER lumen as it is synthesized.

### 2.2 Smooth ER (SER)

Lacking ribosomes, the SER is specialized in:
- **Lipid synthesis** (phospholipids, cholesterol, steroids)
- **Detoxification** (liver: cytochrome P450)
- **Ca²⁺ storage** (crucial for muscle contraction)

## 3. The Golgi Apparatus: The Cellular Post Office

The Golgi apparatus is a set of flattened membrane cisternae (**saccules**) organized in a polarized stack:

- **Cis face** (entry): receives vesicles from the ER
- **Medial region**: post-translational modifications (glycosylation, phosphorylation, sulfation)
- **Trans face** (exit): sorts and dispatches proteins to their final destinations

<MetaNote title="The Mannose-6-Phosphate Code">
  Lysosomal proteins receive a specific tag at the Golgi — the addition of **mannose-6-phosphate** — which serves as their "zip code" to be sorted into lysosomes. Without this tag, they are mistakenly secreted (I-cell disease).
</MetaNote>

## 4. Mitochondria: The Energy Powerhouses

Mitochondria are **double-membrane** organelles (~0.5-3 μm) that produce the majority of **cellular ATP** (via oxidative phosphorylation).

### 4.1 Structure and Compartments

| Compartment | Membrane | Key Functions |
|-------------|---------|--------------|
| **Matrix** | — | Krebs cycle, β-oxidation, mitochondrial DNA |
| **Intermembrane space** | Between 2 membranes | Proton accumulation (pH gradient) |
| **Inner membrane** | Highly folded (cristae) | Respiratory chain, ATP synthase |
| **Outer membrane** | Porins (VDAC) | Permeable to small molecules |

### 4.2 Endosymbiotic Theory

Mitochondria descended from an **α-proteobacterium** engulfed ~1.5 billion years ago. Evidence:
- **Own genome** (circular DNA, ~37 genes)
- **Prokaryotic-type ribosomes** (70S, inhibited by antibiotics)
- **Double membrane** (inner membrane = ancient bacterial membrane)
- **Independent replication** by binary fission

## 5. Lysosomes: The Recycling Centers

Lysosomes are vesicles containing more than **50 acid hydrolases** (optimal pH ~4.5-5) capable of degrading all biological macromolecules. They are involved in:

- **Autophagy**: degradation of damaged cellular organelles
- **Heterophagy**: digestion of extracellular materials (bacteria)
- **Programmed cell death** (secondary lysis)

> The lysosomal membrane is protected from self-digestion by a thick coat of **glycosylated glycoproteins** (luminal side) and by maintaining a V-ATPase proton pump that acidifies the lumen.

## 6. The Cytoskeleton: Framework and Mobility

The cytoskeleton is a dynamic network of protein filaments that determines cell shape, enables motility, and organizes organelles in space.

| Component | Diameter | Protein | Main Function |
|-----------|---------|---------|--------------|
| **Actin microfilaments** | ~7 nm | G/F-actin | Cell shape, amoeboid movement, cytokinesis |
| **Intermediate filaments** | ~10 nm | Keratin, vimentin, lamins | Mechanical resistance |
| **Microtubules** | ~25 nm | α/β-tubulin | Vesicular transport, mitotic spindle, cilia and flagella |

<MetaNote title="Molecular Motors: Kinesins and Dyneins">
  Vesicular transport along microtubules is carried out by **molecular motors**: **kinesins** move toward the (+) pole (periphery) and **dyneins** toward the (−) pole (centrosome). These molecular machines consume ATP to take ~8 nm steps per hydrolysis.
</MetaNote>
