# Lesson 1: The Plasma Membrane and the Fluid Mosaic Model

The plasma membrane is the living boundary of the cell — a dynamic, selective barrier that defines the cellular "self" and governs all exchanges with the extracellular environment. Far from being a simple passive envelope, it constitutes a functional system of remarkable sophistication.

<SelfEval title="The Plasma Membrane" type="pre" />

## 1. Biochemical Composition of the Membrane

The plasma membrane is essentially composed of **phospholipids**, **proteins**, and **cholesterol**. Its structure is described by the **Fluid Mosaic Model** (Singer & Nicolson, 1972).

### 1.1 Phospholipids: The Basic Architecture

A phospholipid is an **amphipathic** molecule — it has a hydrophilic head (polar, water-loving) and two hydrophobic tails (nonpolar, water-fearing). Spontaneously, in an aqueous environment, these molecules self-assemble into a **bilayer**:

- **Outer and inner leaflets**: phosphate heads face the aqueous environments (cytosol and extracellular fluid)
- **Hydrophobic core**: fatty acid tails form a core impermeable to polar molecules and ions

<MetaNote title="Why a Bilayer and Not a Micelle?">
  Phospholipids form a bilayer (rather than a spherical micelle) because their two tails impose a cylindrical geometry, favoring planar membranes. Detergents, having only one tail, form micelles.
</MetaNote>

### 1.2 Cholesterol: The Fluidity Regulator

Cholesterol is intercalated between phospholipids. Its role is dual and paradoxical:

- **At high temperature**: reduces fluidity by limiting phospholipid movement (rigidifying effect)
- **At low temperature**: prevents membrane solidification (fluidizing effect) by interposing between fatty acid tails

> This thermoregulatory action of cholesterol is essential for cell survival in thermally variable environments.

### 1.3 Membrane Proteins: Functional Diversity

Proteins represent approximately **50% of membrane mass** and fall into two major categories:

| Type | Location | Primary Function | Examples |
|------|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Integral (transmembrane)** | Span the entire bilayer | Transport, signal reception | Ion channels, GPCRs, transporters |
| **Peripheral** | Associated with inner or outer surface | Signaling, cytoskeleton anchoring | Ankyrin, spectrin |

<MetaNote title="Transmembrane Domains as α-Helices">
  Integral proteins cross the membrane via α-helix segments rich in hydrophobic amino acids (leucine, valine, isoleucine). These 20-25 amino acids form a stable anchor within the lipid core.
</MetaNote>

## 2. The Fluid Mosaic Model: Key Principles

The model describes the membrane as a **two-dimensional fluid** in which proteins float — like a "living puzzle" in perpetual motion.

### 2.1 Membrane Fluidity

Phospholipids are in constant motion:
- **Lateral diffusion**: rapid (μs), phospholipids slide within the same leaflet (~2 μm/s)
- **Flip-flop (transverse)**: extremely slow (hours to days), passage from one leaflet to the other — requires enzymes (flippases, floppases, scramblases)

**Factors that increase fluidity:**
- Presence of **unsaturated** fatty acids (cis-double bonds create kinks)
- **High temperature**
- **Low** cholesterol content (at low temperature)

**Factors that decrease fluidity:**
- **Saturated** fatty acids (straight tails, dense stacking)
- **Low temperature**
- **High** cholesterol content (at high temperature)

### 2.2 Membrane Asymmetry

The two leaflets are **not identical** — the membrane is asymmetric:

- **Outer leaflet**: phosphatidylcholine, sphingomyelin, glycolipids
- **Inner (cytosolic) leaflet**: phosphatidylserine (negatively charged), phosphatidylinositol (role in signaling)

> Phosphatidylserine in the inner leaflet is an apoptotic signal: when it appears in the outer leaflet, it signals macrophages that the cell is undergoing programmed death.

## 3. Selective Permeability: What Passes, What Doesn't

The lipid bilayer is **impermeable** to large polar molecules and ions, but **permeable** to small nonpolar molecules.

### 3.1 Permeability Rules

| Molecule Category | Permeability | Passage Mechanism |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂, N₂) | ✅ Very high | Simple (free) diffusion |
| Small uncharged polar molecules (H₂O, ethanol, glycerol) | ⚠️ Low to moderate | Simple diffusion or aquaporins |
| Ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺) | ❌ Near zero | Ion channels, pumps |
| Large molecules (glucose, amino acids) | ❌ None | Specific transporters |

### 3.2 Active vs. Passive Transport

**Passive transport** (no energy required, follows the gradient):
- **Simple diffusion**: nonpolar molecules directly through the bilayer
- **Facilitated diffusion**: polar molecules or ions via transport proteins (channels, carriers)

**Active transport** (consumes ATP, against the gradient):
- **Primary**: ATPase pumps (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, proton pump)
- **Secondary**: cotransporters that exploit the gradient created by primary transport (SGLT1: Na⁺/glucose symporter)

<MetaNote title="The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase Pump: A Fundamental Example">
  For each ATP hydrolyzed, this pump expels **3 Na⁺** out of the cell and imports **2 K⁺** inside. It maintains the membrane potential and accounts for ~25% of total cellular energy expenditure at rest.
</MetaNote>

## 4. Lipid Rafts

The membrane is not a homogeneous fluid — it contains **microdomains enriched in cholesterol and sphingolipids** called **lipid rafts**. These more ordered, less fluid zones serve as **signaling platforms**, clustering receptors and signaling proteins to optimize signal transduction cascade efficiency.
