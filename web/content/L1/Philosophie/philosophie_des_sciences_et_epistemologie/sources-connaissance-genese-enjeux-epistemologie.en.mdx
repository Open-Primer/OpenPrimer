## Introduction: What is Epistemology?
Epistemology, from the ancient Greek *epistēmē* (knowledge, science) and *logos* (discourse, study), is the branch of philosophy dedicated to the critical study of human knowledge. It questions the nature, origin, scope, and limits of what we claim to know. Far from being a mere accumulation of facts, knowledge is a complex process that raises fundamental questions: what does it truly mean to know? How can our beliefs be justified? What is the difference between opinion and true knowledge? Can absolute certainty be achieved?

This discipline is not limited to scientific knowledge alone, although it is a privileged field of application, as Dominique Lecourt emphasizes <Citation refNum={1} quote="La science n'est pas une simple accumulation de faits, mais une construction historique et sociale, toujours en dialogue avec son époque et ses idéologies." author="Dominique Lecourt" source="Prométhée, Faust, Frankenstein: Fondements imaginaires de l'éthique" year="1996" original="La science n'est pas une simple accumulation de faits, mais une construction historique et sociale, toujours en dialogue avec son époque et ses idéologies." commentary="Cette citation synthétise l'approche critique de Lecourt envers la philosophie des sciences, soulignant que la connaissance scientifique n'est pas un reflet neutre et objectif de la réalité, mais qu'elle est profondément ancrée dans des contextes historiques, sociaux et idéologiques. Il conteste les conceptions positivistes qui présentent la science comme une progression linéaire de découvertes, plaidant plutôt pour une compréhension plus nuancée de son développement, marqué par des discontinuités, des ruptures conceptuelles et l'influence de forces sociales plus larges. Cette perspective s'inscrit dans la tradition épistémologique française, notamment les travaux de Gaston Bachelard et Georges Canguilhem, qui ont souligné le rôle des ruptures épistémologiques et la constitution historique des objets scientifiques."></Citation>. It is interested in all forms of knowledge, whether empirical, rational, intuitive, or even metaphysical. Throughout this course, we will explore the major stages of epistemological thought, from Plato's foundational questions on the nature of truth and the distinction between opinion and knowledge, to Kant's modern reflections on the conditions of possibility of knowledge, passing through scientific revolutions and contemporary debates on the scientific method. This historical journey will allow us to grasp the crucial issues related to the validity, objectivity, and progression of our knowledge.

## The Ancient Roots of Knowledge: Plato and the Theory of Forms

The quest for knowledge and truth finds its first systematic formulations in ancient Greek philosophy, particularly with Plato (circa 428-348 BCE). For Plato, true knowledge (*epistēmē*) cannot be found in the sensible world, that of our changing and imperfect perceptions. This world is merely a pale copy of a superior and immutable reality: the world of Ideas or intelligible Forms.

The Platonic conception of knowledge is that of **recollection** (*anamnesis*). According to him, the soul, before incarnating in a body, directly contemplated these perfect Ideas. To learn, therefore, is to remember, to rediscover these eternal truths already present within us. Access to this authentic knowledge is emblematically illustrated by the **Allegory of the Cave**, presented in Book VII of *La République*. The prisoners, chained and seeing only shadows projected on the wall, symbolize humanity trapped in the world of appearances and **opinion** (*doxa*). The liberation of a prisoner and his ascent out of the cave represent the philosophical journey towards the contemplation of the Ideas, towards the light of truth and knowledge.

Plato thus establishes a fundamental distinction between opinion (*doxa*), which is uncertain, changing, and linked to the sensible world, and knowledge (*epistēmē*), which is certain, universal, and based on the contemplation of Ideas. The central challenge for Plato is to attain a truth and certainty that are not subject to error or the contingency of the material world. Only reason, through dialectic and intellectual contemplation, can guide us towards this authentic knowledge, allowing us to distinguish reality from appearance, and true knowledge from mere belief.

After exploring the Platonic foundations of the distinction between opinion and knowledge, the journey of epistemological thought leads us to the upheavals of modernity, where new questions about the origin and validity of knowledge gave rise to major philosophical currents.

## The Great Modern Debate: Rationalism and Empiricism

The 17th century marks a decisive turning point in the philosophy of knowledge, characterized by a renewed quest for certainty in the face of the era's uncertainties. Two dominant schools of thought emerged, proposing radically different paths to access truth: rationalism and empiricism.

**Rationalism**, of which René Descartes (1596-1650) is the emblematic figure, postulates that reason is the primary source of all valid knowledge. Faced with the multiplicity of opinions and the lack of solid foundation in the sciences of his time, Descartes undertook a radical approach: **methodical doubt**. This involves rejecting as false anything that can be doubted, in order to find an indubitable truth upon which to build certain knowledge. This doubt applies to the senses, often deceptive, and even to mathematical truths, under the hypothesis of an "evil demon". The outcome of this doubt is the discovery of *Cogito, ergo sum* ("I think, therefore I am"). The very act of doubting proves the existence of the thinking subject. This first truth, clear and distinct, becomes the starting point for deducing other truths, such as the existence of God and the external world, from innate ideas and logic. For rationalists, authentic knowledge is that which is universal, necessary, and logically deduced from first principles, accessible solely by the natural light of reason.

In opposition, **empiricism**, represented by thinkers such as John Locke (1632-1704) and David Hume (1711-1776), asserts that all knowledge derives from sensory experience. For Locke, the human mind at birth is a *tabula rasa*, a "blank slate", devoid of innate ideas. All our ideas come either from sensation (external experience) or from reflection (internal experience of our own mental operations). Knowledge is constructed by the assembly and combination of these "simple ideas" into "complex ideas".

David Hume pushed empiricism to its most skeptical consequences. He distinguishes "impressions" (vivid and immediate perceptions, such as color or sound) from "ideas" (faint copies of these impressions). For Hume, any idea that cannot be traced back to a corresponding impression is meaningless. This approach led him to question the notion of **causality**. We observe a constant succession of events (for example, fire precedes heat), but we never observe the "necessary connection" between them. Our belief in causality is not a logical truth or a sensory impression, but a mere mental habit, a psychological expectation formed by the repetition of past experiences. This problem of **induction** highlights the limits of empirical knowledge: how can we justify the validity of scientific laws that generalize from past observations to predict the future, if causality is merely a habit? Humean skepticism shook the foundations of science and metaphysics of his time.

## Kant's Critical Synthesis: The Conditions of Possibility for Knowledge

Faced with the apparent impasse between dogmatic rationalism and empiricist skepticism, Emmanuel Kant (1724-1804) proposed a new path, which he himself called a "Copernican revolution" in epistemology. Rather than assuming that our mind conforms to objects to know them, Kant suggested that objects must conform to our mind to be known. Knowledge is not a passive reception of information from the external world, but an active construction of the mind.

## Conclusion
The Kantian synthesis, by redefining the terms of the debate between rationalism and empiricism, marked a decisive turning point, laying the foundations for modern epistemology. The journey we have outlined, from Platonic inquiries into the nature of truth and justification, through the Cartesian quest for indubitable certainty, to the Humean challenge of skepticism and Kant's critical response, reveals a constant: philosophy has tirelessly focused on the conditions of possibility, the validity, and the limits of knowledge. These foundational stages have highlighted the central problems of epistemology: how to distinguish an opinion from true knowledge? What is the basis for the certainty of our assertions? How can we justify our beliefs so that they attain the status of knowledge? These questions, far from being resolved, have formed the fertile ground upon which all subsequent philosophy of knowledge, including the philosophy of science, has developed.

The legacy of these debates is immense and continues to inform contemporary thought. Reflections on the nature of truth, epistemic justification, and the quest for certainty have profoundly influenced 20th-century epistemologists, who in turn questioned the specificity of scientific knowledge. Figures such as Gaston Bachelard, Karl Popper, Thomas Kuhn, or Paul Feyerabend, though divergent in their approaches, all inherited these fundamental problems to analyze the dynamics of science. Their works, often in critical dialogue with classical foundations (as Alan Chalmers highlights), shifted attention from individual knowledge to the collective and historical practices of science, questioning scientific rationality, the role of paradigms and revolutions, or the place of error and anarchy in the progress of knowledge.

Here is an overview of the major contributions of some of these 20th-century epistemologists:

| Epistemologist | Key Idea(s) | Impact on the understanding of science |
| :------------ | :------------ | :-------------------------------------- |
| Gaston Bachelard | Epistemological obstacles, epistemological break, philosophy of the "no" | Emphasizes the discontinuity of scientific progress and the necessity of overcoming prejudices to access objective knowledge. |
| Karl Popper | Falsifiability, critical rationalism, open science | Proposes falsifiability as a criterion for scientific demarcation and a critical approach to knowledge, where theories are constantly tested and refuted. |
| Thomas Kuhn | Paradigms, normal science, scientific revolutions, incommensurability | Describes science as evolving through periods of stability (normal science within a paradigm) interrupted by crises and revolutions that radically change the worldview. |
| Paul Feyerabend | Epistemological anarchism, "anything goes" | Criticizes rigid scientific methods, advocating methodological freedom for scientific progress, arguing that no universal and superior method exists. |

Today, epistemology faces new challenges: the explosion of data, artificial intelligence, the complexity of scientific systems, the issue of post-truth and disinformation. It must also integrate contributions from cognitive sciences and neurosciences, while continuing to question the nature of objectivity and the relationship between knowledge and power. Epistemology thus remains a dynamic and essential field of research for understanding our relationship to the world and to knowledge.

### Glossary

- **Demarcation criterion** : Principle or set of principles allowing to distinguish science from non-science (or pseudo-science). Karl Popper notably proposed falsifiability as a demarcation criterion.
- **Epistemology** : Branch of philosophy that studies scientific knowledge, its methods, its foundations, its validity, and its limits. It questions what science is and how it produces reliable knowledge.
- **Falsifiability** : Concept introduced by Karl Popper, according to which a scientific theory must be potentially refutable by experience or observation. A non-falsifiable theory is not considered scientific.
- **Paradigm** : According to Thomas Kuhn, a set of theories, methods, values, and practices shared by a scientific community at a given time, which guides research and problem-solving.
