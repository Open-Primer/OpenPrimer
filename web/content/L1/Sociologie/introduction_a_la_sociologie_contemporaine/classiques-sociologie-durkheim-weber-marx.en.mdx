## Introduction: Why Study the Classics of Sociology?
Welcome to this introductory course on contemporary sociology. To understand current social dynamics, it is imperative to delve into the foundations of this discipline. This lesson is dedicated to the "classics" of sociology: Émile Durkheim, Max Weber, and Karl Marx. These thinkers of the 19th and early 20th centuries are not mere historical figures; their works constitute the pillars upon which modern sociology has been built and continues to reflect.

Their importance lies in the fact that they posed the fundamental questions that still drive sociological research today: How does society hold together? What motivates individual and collective action? What are the drivers of social change? How are power and inequalities structured? By offering distinct conceptual and methodological frameworks, they laid the foundations for the major sociological paradigms. Their thought is an essential starting point for any rigorous sociological analysis.

Understanding Durkheim, Weber, and Marx means acquiring the necessary tools to analyze contemporary social phenomena, whether it be social cohesion, economic transformations, identity movements, or the challenges of modernity. Their concepts, although formulated in a specific historical context, still resonate in our globalized and complex societies.

In this lesson, we will successively explore the major contributions of each of these authors. We will begin with Émile Durkheim and his vision of society as a *sui generis* reality and his quest for the foundations of social cohesion. Next, we will address Max Weber, who emphasized the meaning of social action and the rationalization of the world. Finally, we will study Karl Marx, whose analysis of production relations and class conflicts profoundly influenced the understanding of economic and social structures.

## Émile Durkheim: The Social Fact and Social Cohesion
Émile Durkheim (1858-1917) is often considered one of the founding fathers of French sociology and of sociology as an autonomous science. His work is a rigorous attempt to establish sociology as a scientific discipline distinct from philosophy and psychology, endowed with its own object of study and its own method.

At the heart of Durkheimian thought lies the concept of **social fact**. In his fundamental work, *Les Règles de la méthode sociologique*, Durkheim defines the social fact as "any way of acting, fixed or not, capable of exercising external constraint on the individual; or again, which is general throughout a given society while having an existence of its own, independent of its individual manifestations" . This definition highlights three essential characteristics of the social fact:
* **Externality**: Social facts exist outside individual consciousnesses. They are not created by the individual but pre-exist and outlive them.
* **Constraint**: They impose themselves on the individual through coercive power, whether formal (laws, sanctions) or informal (norms, customs, fashions). The individual often conforms without even being aware of it.
* **Generality**: They are common to most members of a given society, or at least to a specific social group.

For Durkheim, society is not a mere sum of individuals; it is a specific reality, a collective entity endowed with emergent properties that cannot be reduced to the characteristics of its members. This is what he calls the *sui generis* reality of society. The object of sociology is therefore the study of these social facts, treated as observable and measurable "things".

Durkheim was particularly interested in the question of **social cohesion** and the mechanisms that ensure the integration of individuals within the collective. He analyzed the **social division of labor** not only as an economic phenomenon but especially as a factor of solidarity. He distinguishes two types of solidarity:
* **Mechanical solidarity**: Characteristic of traditional, undifferentiated societies. It is based on the similarity of individuals, who share the same beliefs, values, and ways of life (the "collective consciousness"). Cohesion is strong because individuals resemble each other.
* **Organic solidarity**: Characteristic of modern, industrialized, and highly differentiated societies. It is based on the complementarity and interdependence of individuals, each having a specialized function. Cohesion is ensured by the mutual need of one for another.

To better understand these two forms of solidarity, here is a comparative table:

| Characteristic | Mechanical Solidarity | Organic Solidarity |
| :-------------- | :---------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------ |
| **Type of society** | Traditional, little differentiated | Modern, industrialized, highly differentiated |
| **Basis of cohesion** | Similarity of individuals, strong collective consciousness | Complementarity, interdependence of functions |
| **Nature of ties** | Strong, direct, based on resemblance | Specialized, indirect, based on mutual necessity |
| **Predominant law** | Repression (criminal law) | Restitution (civil, commercial law) |
| **Main risk** | Low individualization, excessive conformity | Anomie (deregulation of norms), isolation |

However, the transition to organic solidarity can lead to dysfunctions. Durkheim introduced the concept of **anomie** to describe a state of social deregulation where collective norms and values weaken or disappear, leaving individuals without bearings. This absence of moral regulation can lead to a feeling of disorientation and isolation, and Durkheim notably studied it in his analysis of suicide, showing how social factors can influence seemingly individual acts.

The Durkheimian method is **holistic**: it favors the explanation of social phenomena by other social facts, and not by individual motivations or psychological factors. For Durkheim, society takes precedence over the individual, and it is by studying social structures and constraints that individual behaviors can be understood.

In contrast to Émile Durkheim's holistic perspective, which emphasizes the constraint of social facts on the individual, Max Weber's (1864-1920) sociology adopts a resolutely **individualistic and interpretive** approach. For Weber, the central object of sociology is to understand the meaning that individuals give to their actions, rather than to postulate the existence of a supra-individual collective consciousness. His method aims to grasp the subjective meaning of human conduct, an approach he calls the *Verstehen* (understanding).

## Max Weber: Social Action and the Rationalization of the World
The cornerstone of Weberian sociology is the concept of **social action**. He defines it as human behavior to which the actor attaches a subjective meaning and which is oriented towards the behavior of others. A simple bicycle collision is not a social action, but a subsequent altercation, where individuals react to each other based on their intentions and perceptions, is one. Weber distinguishes four ideal types of social action, which he conceptualized as analytical tools:

1. **Instrumentally rational action (zweckrational):** The actor rationally evaluates means in relation to a given end, weighing costs and benefits. For example, an engineer designing a bridge to minimize costs and maximize resistance.
2. **Value-rational action (wertrational):** The actor acts according to an ethical, aesthetic, religious, or other conviction, which they consider to have intrinsic value, regardless of the consequences. For example, a captain who sinks with his ship out of honor.
3. **Affective action (affektuell):** The action is dictated by the emotions or feelings of the moment, without prior reflection. For example, a slap given in a fit of anger.
4. **Traditional action (traditional):** The action is guided by habit, custom, acquired reflexes. For example, daily rituals or customary greetings.

To better understand these distinctions, the table below summarizes the characteristics of each type of social action:

| Type of Social Action | Main Characteristic | Example |
| :-------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Instrumentally Rational** | Calculation of means to achieve a specific end. | An entrepreneur optimizing production to maximize profits. |
| **Value-Rational** | Acting according to ethical, aesthetic, or religious convictions. | A political activist who refuses any compromise on their principles, even if it harms their election. |
| **Affective** | Dictated by emotions or feelings of the moment. | An outburst of joy or anger in reaction to an unexpected event. |
| **Traditional** | Guided by habit, custom, or acquired reflexes. | Celebrating an annual festival according to ancestral rituals or formally greeting a superior. |

An illustration of the different types of social action according to Max Weber.

These types are not rigid categories of reality, but **ideal types**. The **ideal type** is an essential methodological tool for Weber. It is an abstract conceptual construction, a unilateral accentuation of certain features of reality, which does not perfectly correspond to a concrete situation but serves as a compass for analysis. It allows comparing observed reality to this purified model to grasp its specificities and deviations, thus constituting an indispensable guide for sociological analysis.

Beyond individual action, Weber was interested in major historical trends, particularly the process of **rationalization** that characterizes modern Western societies. Rationalization is the progressive extension of instrumental rationality (instrumentally rational action) to all areas of social life (economy, law, administration, science, art). It manifests itself through the search for efficiency, predictability, calculability, and control.

This rationalization inevitably leads to the **disenchantment of the world** (*Entzauberung der Welt*). Magical, religious, or traditional explanations of the world are supplanted by science, technology, and logic. The world loses its mystical and sacred dimension to become an object of calculation and mastery. Although a source of progress and freedom, this process can also trap the individual in a bureaucratic and dehumanizing "iron cage".

Weber's method is therefore **individualistic**: it starts from the individual and the meaning they give to their action to understand collective social phenomena. Its object of study is the **meaning of individual action**, and it is by aggregating and confronting these meanings that social structures and dynamics can be analyzed.
## Karl Marx: Social Classes, Alienation, and Social Transformation

While Durkheim focuses on social integration and Weber on the meaning of action, Karl Marx (1818-1883) offers a radically different perspective, centered on production relations, class conflicts, and social transformation. His thought is rooted in a **historical materialist theory**, according to which the history of societies is determined by the material conditions of existence, i.e., the way in which humans produce their means of subsistence. For Marx, it is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but their social being that determines their consciousness.

At the heart of this theory are **social classes**. Marx defines them not by income or status, but by their position in production relations. In capitalist society, he primarily distinguishes two antagonistic classes:
* The **bourgeoisie**, which owns the means of production (factories, land, capital).
* The **proletariat**, which owns only its labor power and is forced to sell it to the bourgeoisie to subsist.

The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of **class struggle**. This incessant conflict between dominant and dominated classes is the fundamental engine of social change and historical evolution. Each era is marked by a specific form of class struggle, which inevitably leads to revolution and the emergence of a new mode of production.

In the capitalist system, Marx identifies the phenomenon of **aliénation**. Alienation describes the loss of control of the individual over their labor, the product of their labor, their relationships with others, and their own human nature. Work, which should be a source of fulfillment, becomes a means of survival, devoid of meaning for the worker who no longer recognizes themselves in what they produce. They are separated from the fruit of their labor, which becomes a commodity belonging to the capitalist.

A schematic representation of the Marxist concepts of alienation and class struggle.

**Ideology** is another key concept for Marx. It refers to a system of ideas, values, and beliefs that masks real relations of domination and production. The dominant ideology is always the ideology of the dominant class; it justifies the existing social order and helps maintain the "false consciousness" of the exploited classes, preventing them from becoming aware of their own exploitation and class interests.

Marx's method is **dialectical**. Inspired by Hegel, it postulates that social change results from the confrontation of opposing forces (thesis and antithesis) that resolve into a new synthesis. For Marx, this dialectic is materialistic: it applies to productive forces and production relations, not to ideas. Marx's object of study is therefore the analysis of **production relations** and the **dynamics of social change** they generate, with the aim of understanding and transforming the world.

## Cross-Perspectives: Methods, Objects, and Legacies of the Classics
The study of the works of Durkheim, Weber, and Marx reveals fundamental divergences in their methodological approaches, their preferred objects of study, and their underlying visions of society. These differences are not mere nuances, but epistemological choices that have structured subsequent sociological debates and schools of thought.

On the **methodological** level, Durkheim adopts a **holistic** and positivist stance, insisting on the necessity of treating social facts as things, observable and measurable from the outside. His method is comparative and statistical, seeking to identify general laws and correlations between social phenomena. Conversely, Weber promotes **methodological individualism**, seeking to understand the meaning that actors give to their social actions. His method is interpretive (*Verstehen*) and relies on the construction of "ideal types" to analyze the rationality of behaviors and organizations. Marx, for his part, uses a **dialectical and materialist** approach, analyzing the inherent contradictions in production relations and the historical dynamics of social transformation. His method aims both to understand and to transform the world, by revealing the mechanisms of exploitation and alienation.

The **objects of study** of these three thinkers reflect their methodologies. Durkheim focuses on phenomena of **social cohesion**, solidarity, regulation, and integration, as well as social pathologies like anomie. He is interested in institutions, norms, and collective values that form the social bond. Weber explores **meaningful social actions**, forms of legitimate domination (traditional, charismatic, legal-rational), bureaucracy, and the influence of value systems (especially religious) on economic and social development. Marx, for his part, places at the center of his analysis **production relations**, the structure of social classes, the mechanisms of capitalist exploitation, alienation, and ideology, seeking to decipher the laws of the historical movement of societies.

Their **visions of society** are also distinct. For Durkheim, society is a *sui generis* reality, a moral system that takes precedence over the individual and ensures their socialization. He sees society as an organism whose parts contribute to the balance and function of the whole. Weber perceives society as the aggregated result of meaningful individual actions, but also as a process of increasing rationalization, leading to a bureaucratic "iron cage." Marx envisions society as a battlefield, a place of inevitable **class struggle**, where economic contradictions lead to revolutions and changes in modes of production.

Despite their divergences, the contributions of Durkheim, Weber, and Marx are immense and complementary. Durkheim founded sociology as an autonomous scientific discipline, emphasizing the importance of social facts and cohesion. His work inspired **structural functionalism** and the study of institutions. Weber enriched sociology with an interpretive dimension and a fine analysis of individual motivations and forms of power, influencing the sociology of organizations, political sociology, and cultural sociology. Marx offered a radical critique of capitalism and a powerful theory of inequalities and conflicts, laying the foundations for **critical theories** and the sociology of classes.

Their **limitations** are also recognized. Durkheim is sometimes criticized for his social determinism and a certain neglect of individual agency. Weber can be seen as too focused on rationality and intentional action, underestimating structural constraints. Marx is often reproached for his economic determinism and a teleological view of history. However, these "limitations" are also the starting points for new research and theoretical syntheses, such as that proposed by Pierre Bourdieu, who integrates elements from these three traditions to analyze social fields and forms of capital. Others, like Anthony Giddens, have sought to overcome the structure/agency opposition by proposing theories of structuration.

A schematic illustration of the interconnections and influences of the three classics on contemporary sociological thought.

Their influence is omnipresent in contemporary sociology. Questions of social cohesion, regulation, and deviance (Durkheim), rationalization, power, and bureaucracy (Weber), or inequalities, conflicts, and social transformation (Marx) remain at the heart of sociological concerns. Thinkers like Michel Foucault are part of a critical lineage that owes much to Marx and Weber in the analysis of power relations and institutions. Urban sociology, sociology of work, sociology of religions, political sociology, and many other sub-disciplines continue to engage with the legacies of these founders.

## Conclusion
In summary, Émile Durkheim, Max Weber, and Karl Marx, each in their own way, laid the groundwork for sociology as a scientific discipline, offering fundamental analytical frameworks for understanding the social world. Durkheim taught us to see society as a constraining and moral reality, essential for the cohesion and integration of individuals. Weber revealed the complexity of social actions, forms of power, and the inevitable process of rationalization that shapes our modern societies. Marx highlighted the dynamics of conflict, exploitation, and alienation inherent in economic structures, particularly capitalism. Their works, though born in specific historical contexts, are not mere relics of the past; they constitute indispensable conceptual and methodological tools for deciphering the challenges and transformations of our current societies.

The relevance of their analyses is striking in the face of contemporary issues. Questions of social polarization, fragmentation of collective ties, and the search for meaning in a changing world resonate with Durkheimian concerns about anomie and solidarity. The rise of populism, the increasing bureaucratization of public and private spheres, and the impact of ethics on the economy find deep echoes in Weber's work on domination and rationalization. Finally, the increase in global inequalities, the precariousness of work, recurrent economic crises, and protest social movements confirm the power of Marxist analysis of class relations and capitalism. Contemporary sociology cannot ignore these legacies; it questions them, critiques them, combines them, and transcends them to build new understandings of the social. It is enriched by this diversity of approaches, whether they are in continuity with these classics or propose paradigmatic ruptures, testifying to the vitality and complexity of the discipline.

### Glossary

- **Alienation** : For Marx, the process by which the individual is dispossessed of their labor, its products, their human essence, and their ability to realize themselves, particularly within the framework of capitalism.
- **Anomie** : A Durkheimian concept designating a state of social deregulation where collective norms and values are weakened or absent, leading to individual disorientation.
- **Class Struggle** : A central concept for Marx, designating the inherent conflict between different social classes (notably bourgeoisie and proletariat) whose interests are antagonistic, the engine of history.
- **Ideal Type** : Weber's methodological tool, an abstract and unilateral conceptual construction that accentuates certain characteristics of reality to facilitate its analysis and comparison.
- **Rationalization** : A historical process described by Weber, characterized by the extension of instrumental and calculating rationality to all areas of social life, leading to bureaucratization and the disenchantment of the world.
- **Social Fact** : According to Durkheim, any way of acting, thinking, and feeling, external to the individual, and endowed with a power of coercion by virtue of which it imposes itself on them.
