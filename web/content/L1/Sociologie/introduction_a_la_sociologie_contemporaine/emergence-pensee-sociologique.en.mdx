## Introduction: Why a Science of Society?

This course aims to explore the conditions for the emergence of sociology as an autonomous scientific discipline. Far from being a spontaneous creation, sociology is the result of a complex historical process, rooted in major upheavals that redefined European society in the 18th and 19th centuries. The central question that will guide us is: why did a science dedicated to the systematic study of the social emerge precisely at this moment in history, and with what epistemological and methodological specificities did it distinguish itself from earlier reflections on man and society?

To answer these fundamental questions, this lesson will be structured in three parts. We will begin by examining the socio-historical and intellectual context of the 18th and 19th centuries, a period of profound breaks that created an urgent need to understand the social world in a new way. Next, we will address the first attempts at theorizing the social, often stemming from political philosophy or economics, which laid the groundwork for sociological thought. Finally, we will study the foundational figures of sociology and their methodological contributions, which helped consolidate this new science.

## The Socio-Historical and Intellectual Context of the 18th-19th Centuries
The emergence of sociology is inseparable from the profound transformations that shook Europe in the 18th and 19th centuries, creating a sense of urgency and an intellectual need to understand a rapidly changing world. These upheavals, economic, political, and intellectual alike, shook traditional certainties and necessitated a new approach to apprehending the "social."

The **Industrial Revolution**, which began in Great Britain in the 18th century and gradually spread to the European continent, was the driving force behind a radical restructuring of societies. It led to a massive rural exodus and unprecedented **urbanization**, concentrating growing populations in rapidly expanding cities. These new agglomerations became the scene of often unsanitary living and working conditions, generating the **impoverishment** of large segments of the working population. The emergence of large factories and wage labor gave rise to **new social classes**: on the one hand, a rising industrial and financial bourgeoisie, and on the other, an urban proletariat facing exploitation and precariousness. These blatant tensions and inequalities highlighted the "social question," challenging the cohesion and regulation of society.

Concurrently, the **French Revolution of 1789** marked a fundamental political and ideological break. By abolishing the Ancien Régime, its hereditary privileges, and its rigid social structures, it proclaimed the principles of equality, liberty, and popular sovereignty. These upheavals not only dismantled the monarchical and religious order but also sowed doubt about the ability of traditional institutions to maintain social stability. The post-revolutionary period was marked by a succession of political regimes and chronic instability, prompting many thinkers to desire either the restoration of order or, conversely, the construction of a new society on rational foundations. The notion of anomie, or the absence of regulating social norms, would moreover become a key concept for sociologists like Durkheim to analyze these periods of social disintegration.

These social and political transformations unfolded in an intellectual climate characterized by the **rise of scientific thought**. Heir to the Enlightenment and its rationalism, the 19th century saw the triumph of **positivism**, particularly under the impetus of Auguste Comte. This school of thought advocated the application of natural science methods (observation, experimentation, search for laws) to the study of all phenomena, including social phenomena. The idea that society could be studied objectively, that its mechanisms could be discovered, and its problems solved by science, paved the way for a new discipline. Faced with the collapse of religious and metaphysical certainties, and given the scale of social disorders, it became imperative to understand the "social" no longer as an immutable divine or natural order, but as an autonomous object of study, susceptible to scientific analysis and reform. It is in this context of profound crises and the quest for new forms of knowledge that sociology began to take shape, seeking to diagnose the ills of modern society and propose paths for a new social order.

Sociology, as an autonomous scientific discipline, did not emerge in an intellectual vacuum. It was built in rupture with pre-existing forms of thought that already questioned the social, notably social philosophy and political economy.

## Epistemological Breaks: Distinguishing Sociology from its 'Ancestors'
The first fundamental break occurred with **social philosophy**. Traditionally, philosophy often sought to define what society *should be*, by elaborating ideal models or moral principles for a just and harmonious social organization. Its approach was largely **normative and speculative**, relying on pure reason or ethical postulates. Nascent sociology, under the impetus of Auguste Comte's positivism, marked a decisive turning point by orienting itself towards a **descriptive and explanatory** approach. It was no longer about dictating the rules of good governance or the ideal society, but about observing social facts as they are, analyzing them systematically, and deriving their operating laws. This shift from speculation to **empirical observation** and the search for causalities constituted the cornerstone of sociological specificity, seeking to understand the social world rather than to judge it.

The second significant break concerns **political economy**. While the latter had already developed a rigorous analysis of the mechanisms of production, distribution, and exchange, it tended to reduce society to its economic dimensions alone, often postulating a rational and maximizing "homo economicus". Sociology challenged this reductionist view by **broadening the scope of study** beyond mere market laws. It highlighted the importance of non-economic factors – such as culture, religious beliefs, political institutions, kinship ties, norms, and values – in the structuring of societies and the orientation of individual and collective behaviors. Thinkers like Max Weber, for example, showed how religious phenomena could influence economic development. Sociology thus affirmed that the social could not be fully understood without taking into account this multidimensional complexity, insisting on the autonomy and specificity of "social facts" in relation to economic or psychological facts.

The **specificity of the sociological approach** therefore lies in its ambition to constitute a science of the social, endowed with its own investigation methods (survey, statistics, participant observation) and its own concepts (structure, function, role, class, anomie, etc.), in order to analyze the regularities, dynamics, and transformations of human societies in an objective and systematic manner.

To better grasp these foundational distinctions, the following table compares the approaches of these disciplines:

| Characteristic | Social Philosophy | Political Economy | Nascent Sociology |
| :-------------- | :------------------ | :----------------- | :------------------ |
| **Main Objective** | Define what *should be* (normative, ideal) | Analyze mechanisms of production, distribution, and exchange | Understand what *is* (descriptive, explanatory) |
| **Methodology** | Speculation, pure reason, ethical postulates | Theoretical models, market laws, economic rationality | Empirical observation, systematic analysis of social facts |
| **Field of Study** | Social ideals, moral principles, justice | Economic dimensions (market, capital, labor) | Multiple dimensions (culture, religion, politics, family, etc.) |
| **Nature of Facts** | Ideals, values, prescriptions | Economic, rational, individual (homo economicus) | Social, multidimensional, collective, autonomous |
| **Key Question** | How to organize the ideal society? | How to produce and distribute wealth? | How do societies function and transform? |
## Foundational Questions and the First Objects of Study of the Pioneers
The founding fathers of sociology – Auguste Comte, Émile Durkheim, Karl Marx, and Max Weber – although often disagreeing on the answers, shared **major questions** in the face of the upheavals of their era. Raymond Aron brilliantly synthesized these common and divergent concerns. At the heart of their reflections was the question of **social order**: how do societies manage to maintain cohesion in the face of the centrifugal forces of individualism and fragmentation? Durkheim, in particular, focused on **social ties** and the forms of solidarity that unite individuals, seeking to understand how modern societies, characterized by the division of labor, could still cohere.

Concurrently, the question of **social change** was omnipresent. While Comte envisioned a progressive evolution towards a positive state of society, Marx saw **class conflict** as the fundamental engine of history and radical transformations. He highlighted the mechanisms of **alienation** generated by the capitalist system, where the individual is dispossessed of the meaning of their work and their humanity. Weber, for his part, was interested in the increasing **rationalization** of Western societies, a process by which instrumental logic and efficiency calculation permeate all spheres of existence, from economy to administration, leading to a "disenchantment of the world."

These pioneers also laid the foundations for the **method of analyzing** social phenomena. They sought to move beyond individual or psychological explanations to identify collective social determinants. Whether through the study of "social facts" in Durkheim, the analysis of production relations in Marx, or the understanding of "social actions" and "ideal types" in Weber, all worked to equip sociology with rigorous conceptual and methodological tools to grasp the complexity of the social world. Their initial objects of study thus encompassed phenomena as varied as the division of labor, religion, the state, the family, the city, suicide, or social movements, laying the groundwork for a science dedicated to the systematic exploration of collective life.

The table below synthesizes the major contributions of these emblematic figures:

| Pioneer | Main Foundational Question | Key Concept(s) | Method / Focus |
| :------- | :----------------------------- | :----------------- | :-------------- |
| **Auguste Comte** | How does society evolve towards progress and order? | Law of three stages, Positivism, Social statics and dynamics | Observation, classification, search for universal laws |
| **Émile Durkheim** | How to maintain social cohesion in modern societies? | Social fact, Solidarity (mechanical/organic), Anomie, Collective representations | Statistical analysis, study of institutions and social norms |
| **Karl Marx** | What are the drivers of social change and inequality? | Class struggle, Alienation, Infrastructure/Superstructure, Historical materialism | Critique of political economy, analysis of production relations |
| **Max Weber** | How to understand the meaning of social actions and increasing rationalization? | Social action, Ideal type, Disenchantment of the world, Protestant ethic | Interpretive sociology, analysis of motivations and values |

## Conclusion
The emergence of sociology, as we have explored it, is not an isolated phenomenon but the intellectual response to a period of profound upheavals. Industrial and political revolutions, rampant urbanization, and the dissolution of traditional social structures have created an urgent need to understand the new collective dynamics. In this context of crisis and change, sociology asserted itself as a discipline seeking to break with theological, metaphysical, or purely philosophical explanations of the social. It has progressively built its autonomy by equipping itself with a specific object of study – the social fact, social action, production relations – and rigorous methods, aspiring to a scientific rigor comparable to that of the natural sciences, as emphasized by Émile Durkheim in his *Règles de la méthode sociologique* or Max Weber with his interpretive approach. Raymond Aron, moreover, masterfully traced these foundational stages, notably in his work *Les étapes de la pensée sociologique*, which allowed sociology to constitute itself as a science of the social.

This legacy of the pioneers, from Comte to Weber, including Durkheim and Marx, forms the foundation upon which contemporary sociology continues to build. Their fundamental questions about social cohesion, inequality, power, or rationalization remain highly relevant, even if the forms they take have evolved. The discipline, far from being static, has since undergone significant developments, diversifying into multiple currents and specializations to address the complex challenges of the modern world, from globalization to digital transformations, environmental issues, and new forms of identity. It thus continues to offer essential analytical frameworks for deciphering the dynamics of our societies and informing public debates, proving its relevance and adaptability in the face of the incessant changes in the social world.

### Glossary

- **Enlightenment** : An 18th-century European intellectual and cultural movement, characterized by the primacy of reason, the critique of authority (political, religious), and the promotion of individual liberties, scientific progress, and education as means of human emancipation.
- **Industrial Revolution** : A period of profound economic and social changes, beginning in the 18th century in Great Britain, marked by industrialization, massive urbanization, the emergence of new social classes (industrial bourgeoisie, proletariat), and the transformation of modes of production and life.
- **Positivism** : A philosophical current founded by Auguste Comte, which postulates that the only valid knowledge is that which comes from verifiable observation and experience, and which aims to apply the methods of natural sciences (observation, experimentation, classification) to the study of society to discover its laws.
- **Sociology** : A human and social science whose object is the study of social phenomena, human interactions, and organizations, using empirical research methods and explanatory theories to understand the functioning of societies.
