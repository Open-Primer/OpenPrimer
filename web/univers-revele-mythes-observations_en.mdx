# The Universe Revealed: From Myths to First Observations

## Introduction: The Eternal Quest for Understanding the Cosmos

Since the dawn of humanity, the night sky has exerted an uninterrupted fascination, inspiring wonder, prompting profound questions, and stimulating imagination. The twinkling stars, the wandering planets, and the grandiose celestial phenomena – eclipses, comets, aurora borealis – have always been perceived as manifestations of a higher order or mysterious forces, pushing civilizations to elaborate complex narratives to explain their origin, functioning, and meaning. These early attempts, often imbued with myths, deities, and philosophical speculations, laid the foundations for a monumental intellectual quest that would progressively evolve towards an increasingly systematic, empirical, and mathematical approach. Contemporary astrophysics, with its sophisticated models and cutting-edge instruments, is the culmination of millennia of patient observations, audacious reflections, and profound conceptual revolutions.

This course aims to retrace the major steps of this intellectual adventure, from the mythological cosmogonies of ancient civilizations, which sought to give a sacred and anthropocentric meaning to the Universe, to the beginnings of modern astronomy. This transition was marked by the gradual abandonment of supernatural explanations in favor of telescopic observation, mathematical modeling, and the establishment of universal physical laws. We will explore how humanity moved from a Earth-centered view of the cosmos (geocentrism) to an understanding where the Sun occupies a central position (heliocentrism), and then to an apprehension of vast cosmic scales, laying the foundations for our current vision of the Universe as a dynamic system governed by fundamental laws. This historical approach is essential because, as the philosopher of science <RealPerson name="Thomas_Kuhn" lang="fr" bio="Philosophe des sciences américain, célèbre pour son ouvrage 'La Structure des révolutions scientifiques' (1962) qui a introduit le concept de 'paradigme'.">Thomas Kuhn</RealPerson> so eloquently demonstrated in his seminal work *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962), it reveals the progressive, cumulative, and sometimes radically revolutionary nature of scientific knowledge, characterized by paradigm shifts that transform not only our theories but also our very way of perceiving the world [1](#ref-1). Understanding this evolution means grasping the intrinsic dynamic of science and the perseverance of the human spirit in the face of the immensity of the unknown.

## 1. Ancient and Mythological Cosmogonies: First Attempts to Explain Cosmic Order

Before the advent of observational science and the empirical method, human civilizations developed complex systems of thought to make sense of the world around them, including the cosmos. These systems, known as cosmogonies, were often inextricably linked to religion, mythology, philosophy, and social structures. Their primary objective was to explain the origin of the Universe, humanity's place within it, and the divine or primordial forces that governed it. These foundational narratives, transmitted orally and then in writing, served not only to satisfy intellectual curiosity but also to establish a moral, social, and ritual framework for the community. They offered narrative coherence to an otherwise chaotic and unpredictable world.

### 1.1. Founding Myths: The Emergence of Order from Chaos or Nothingness

In most ancient cultures, the Universe is not eternal but proceeds from a primordial state of chaos, nothingness, or a cosmic ocean. From this original state emerge creative deities or primordial forces that shape the world, the heavens, and humanity.

*   **Mesopotamia (Sumer, Akkad, Babylon):** Mesopotamian civilizations, among the first to develop writing and astronomy, elaborated rich and detailed cosmogonies. The Babylonian creation text, the "Enuma Elish" (c. 18th-12th century BCE), describes the creation of the world from the dismembered body of the primordial goddess Tiamat, vanquished by the god Marduk. Heaven and Earth are formed from her halves, and the stars are divine marks or abodes for the gods. Mesopotamian astronomy, though deeply rooted in astrological and divinatory motivations, developed remarkably precise observations. Priest-astronomers, from the tops of ziggurats, cataloged stars, tracked planetary movements, and predicted eclipses with astonishing accuracy, developing a sexagesimal system (base 60) that still influences our measurement of time and angles. Their cuneiform tablets contain detailed records of celestial phenomena, demonstrating a capacity for systematic observation, even if the interpretation remained mythological and oriented towards predicting royal destinies and earthly events [2](#ref-2).

*   **Ancient Egypt:** Egyptian cosmogony is also rich and varied, with different versions depending on the eras and regions (Heliopolis, Hermopolis, Memphis). A recurring theme is the emergence of the world from an undifferentiated primordial ocean, Nun, which represents chaos before creation. The creator god (often Ra, Atum, or Ptah) rises from this ocean, creating the sky (Nut), the Earth (Geb), and the air (Shu), thus separating the elements and establishing order (Ma'at). The daily cycle of the sun, sailing on the solar boat through the sky by day and the underworld by night, is central to their understanding of time, regeneration, and cosmic order. The alignment of many temples and monuments with key astronomical events (solstices, equinoxes, heliacal rising of certain stars) testifies to the importance of celestial observation in their culture and religion.

*   **Ancient Greece (pre-Socratic):** Before the emergence of natural philosophers, poets like <RealPerson name="Hésiode" lang="fr" bio="Poète grec antique, auteur de la 'Théogonie' qui décrit la généalogie des dieux grecs et la création du monde.">Hesiod</RealPerson> offered cosmogonic narratives. In his *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE), Chaos is the first entity, from which emerge Gaia (Earth), Eros (Love), and other primordial deities who give birth to the Olympian pantheon and the world as we know it. This period, however, marks a crucial transition, the passage from *mythos* to *logos*, where thinkers began to seek rational and natural explanations rather than purely divine ones. Philosophers like <RealPerson name="Thalès_de_Milet" lang="fr" bio="Philosophe présocratique, considéré comme le premier philosophe et scientifique grec. Il a proposé que l'eau était le principe originel de toutes choses.">Thales of Miletus</RealPerson> (6th century BCE) proposed that water was the original principle of all things, while <RealPerson name="Anaximandre" lang="fr" bio="Philosophe grec présocratique, disciple de Thalès. Il est connu pour avoir introduit le concept de l'apeiron (l'illimité) comme principe originel de l'univers et pour avoir dessiné la première carte du monde.">Anaximander</RealPerson>, his disciple, introduced the concept of the *apeiron* (the unlimited, the indefinite) as the primordial substance. He also imagined a cylindrical Earth floating at the center of the Universe, surrounded by wheels of fire hidden by rings of air, with openings for the stars, Moon, and Sun. These early attempts, though speculative, marked a break from purely mythological explanations by seeking natural principles.

These cosmogonies share several fundamental characteristics:
*   **Anthropocentrism:** Humanity is often placed at the center of creation or is its ultimate purpose, and the cosmos is perceived as a theater for human and divine affairs.
*   **Deities and supernatural forces:** Supernatural beings, gods, or primordial forces are responsible for the creation, order, and maintenance of the Universe. Their actions explain natural phenomena.
*   **Narrative explanation:** The world is explained by epic tales and divine genealogies rather than by abstract natural laws. These narratives are often symbolic and metaphorical.
*   **Absence of systematic empirical verification:** These systems are not designed to be tested by observation or experimentation in the modern sense. Their validity rests on tradition, revelation, and the internal coherence of the narrative.
*   **Social and ritual function:** Beyond explanation, cosmogonies provide a framework for rituals, festivals, and social structures, linking human destiny to cosmic order.

## 2. The Emergence of Rational Thought: Geocentric Models and the Quest for Celestial Order

With the Greek philosophers, a crucial transition occurred in the way the cosmos was understood: the explanation of the world gradually detached from myths to move towards reason, logic, and systematic observation. Although their models were still far from the precision and complexity of modern science, they introduced the revolutionary idea that the Universe could be understood by the human intellect, through deductive logic and observation, rather than solely by divine revelation. This period marks the birth of natural philosophy, a precursor to science.

### 2.1. Early Greek Models: Spheres, Harmony, and Geometric Perfection

Early Greek thinkers, such as the Milesians, sought material principles to explain the world. However, it was with the Pythagorean, Platonic, and Aristotelian schools that cosmological models achieved geometric and philosophical sophistication. The Pythagoreans, fascinated by numbers and musical harmony, were among the first to postulate a spherical Earth and to imagine a cosmos where celestial bodies moved according to perfect mathematical ratios, producing a "music of the spheres."

*   **Plato (428/427 – 348/347 BCE):** In his dialogue *Timaeus*, Plato describes a cosmos ordered by a Demiurge, a divine intelligence that shapes the world from pre-existing chaos, following ideal and mathematical models. For Plato, the sensible world is merely a pale copy of the world of perfect Forms. The cosmos is therefore intrinsically perfect and geometric. Celestial bodies, symbols of this perfection, move on perfect and concentric spheres around an immobile Earth. Harmony, geometric perfection (especially the circle, the perfect figure par excellence), and regularity are key principles of his cosmology. The task of the astronomer, according to Plato, is not so much to describe what is observed as to "save the phenomena," that is, to find uniform circular motions that explain the apparent and sometimes irregular movements of the planets.

*   **Aristotle (384 – 322 BCE):** A student of Plato, <RealPerson name="Aristote" lang="fr" bio="Philosophe grec antique, élève de Platon et précepteur d'Alexandre le Grand. Ses travaux couvrent une vaste gamme de sujets, de la logique à la métaphysique, en passant par l'éthique, la politique et les sciences naturelles.">Aristotle</RealPerson> systematized the geocentric model, giving it a physical and philosophical basis that would dominate Western thought for nearly two millennia. For Aristotle, the Universe is a closed, finite, and hierarchical system, composed of two distinct regions:
    *   **The sublunary world:** This region, located below the Moon's orbit, includes the Earth and its atmosphere. It is composed of the four elements (earth, water, air, fire), each of which has a natural place and a natural motion (earth and water downwards, air and fire upwards). It is the realm of change, generation, and corruption, of imperfection and linear motion.
    *   **The superlunary world:** Beyond the Moon extends the realm of celestial bodies, composed of a perfect and unalterable fifth element, aether (or quintessence). Celestial bodies (Moon, Sun, planets, fixed stars) are embedded in concentric spheres of aether, rotating uniformly around the Earth, which is the immobile center of the Universe. The natural motion of aether is perfect and eternal circular motion. This model explained the apparent non-variation of the heavens (no birth or death of stars) and the fall of bodies on Earth (natural movement towards the center of the Universe). The sphericity of the Earth was also supported by observations such as the circular shadow of the Earth on the Moon during lunar eclipses and the fact that new stars appear on the horizon when traveling south.

### 2.2. The Ptolemaic Model: Remarkable Precision and Increasing Complexity

The Aristotelian model, though philosophically elegant and coherent, encountered difficulties in explaining certain celestial observations with precision. Planets, in particular, exhibited complex movements: their apparent speed varied, their brightness changed, and above all, they showed "retrograde motions" where they seemed to stop, move backward, and then resume their normal course in the sky. To "save the phenomena" while preserving the principle of geocentrism and circular motions, <RealPerson name="Claude_Ptolémée" lang="fr" bio="Astronome, astrologue, géographe et mathématicien grec d'origine égyptienne, ayant vécu au IIe siècle de notre ère. Son œuvre majeure, l'Almageste, a été le traité d'astronomie de référence pendant plus de 14 siècles, décrivant un modèle géocentrique détaillé de l'Univers.">Claudius Ptolemy</RealPerson> (c. 100 – c. 170 CE), a Greek astronomer, mathematician, and geographer from Alexandria, perfected the geocentric model in his monumental work, the *Almagest* (originally titled *Syntaxis Mathematica*).

<Alert type="biography">
**Claudius Ptolemy (c. 100 – c. 170 CE)** was a major intellectual figure of antiquity, whose influence extended over more than fourteen centuries. Living in Alexandria, the great intellectual center of the time, he synthesized and developed the astronomical, geographical, and mathematical knowledge of his Greek predecessors, notably Hipparchus. His major work, the *Almagest*, is a thirteen-volume treatise on astronomy that served as a reference manual until the Renaissance. In this text, Ptolemy presented a detailed geocentric model of the Universe, integrating ingenious mechanisms such as epicycles, deferents, and equants to explain the complex movements of the planets. This model, remarkably predictive for its time, allowed for the calculation of planetary positions with sufficient accuracy for navigation, astrology, and calendar determination. The *Almagest* profoundly influenced the scientific and philosophical thought of the Islamic world, where it was translated and studied intensively, before returning to medieval Europe to become the cornerstone of astronomy. Ptolemy also wrote influential works on geography (*Geography*), optics (*Optics*), and astrology (*Tetrabiblos*), demonstrating the breadth of his knowledge. His system, though ultimately refuted, represents a pinnacle of ancient intellectual ingenuity in the face of the challenges of celestial observation. Ptolemy introduced several ingenious concepts to make the geocentric model compatible with increasingly precise observations:
*   **Epicycles:** To explain the retrograde motion of planets, Ptolemy postulated that planets do not orbit directly around the Earth, but on small circles called epicycles. The center of this epicycle, in turn, orbits on a larger circle, the deferent, around the Earth. When the planet is on the part of the epicycle that brings it backward relative to the deferent's motion, it appears retrograde from Earth.
*   **Deferents:** These are the large circles on which the centers of the epicycles move. They represent the planet's main trajectory around the Earth.
*   **Eccentrics:** To explain the variations in the apparent speed of planets and changes in their distance from Earth, Ptolemy shifted the center of the deferent slightly away from Earth. Thus, the Earth was not exactly at the center of all orbits, but slightly offset.
*   **Equants:** This was Ptolemy's most complex and controversial innovation. The equant is an imaginary point relative to which the center of the epicycle appeared to move at a constant angular velocity. This allowed for the explanation of observed variations in planetary speed without violating the principle of uniform circular motion, but at the cost of breaking with the idea that motion should be uniform relative to the center of the orbit or the Earth itself.

The Ptolemaic model, though conceptually complex and departing from the perfection of Aristotle's uniform circles, was remarkably effective in its predictions. It allowed for the calculation of planetary positions with sufficient accuracy for the needs of the time and remained the dominant paradigm in astronomy for over 1400 years, being adopted and taught by the Islamic world and medieval Europe. Its longevity testifies to its ingenuity and its ability to "save the phenomena" despite its geocentric hypotheses. However, its increasing complexity, with the addition of ever more epicycles and adjustments to match observations, would eventually make it vulnerable to criticism and pave the way for a new revolution.

## 3. The Heliocentric Revolution: Observing, Calculating, and Questioning the Center of the World

The Ptolemaic model, despite its sophistication and predictive capability, became increasingly difficult to maintain in the face of more precise observations and the growing complexity of calculations needed to adjust epicycles and equants. Renaissance Europe, with its intellectual revival, the rediscovery of ancient Greek texts (including mentions of a heliocentric model by Aristarchus of Samos), and the emergence of a new approach to nature, was ripe for a major scientific revolution. The 16th century marks the beginning of this profound transformation that would overturn the vision of the cosmos and humanity's place within it.

### 3.1. Copernicus and the Death Blow to Geocentrism

<RealPerson name="Nicolas_Copernic" lang="fr" bio="Astronome polonais de la Renaissance, célèbre pour avoir développé la théorie de l'héliocentrisme, selon laquelle la Terre et les autres planètes tournent autour du Soleil. Son œuvre majeure, 'De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium', publiée juste avant sa mort en 1543, a marqué le début de la révolution scientifique.">Nicolaus Copernicus</RealPerson> (1473-1543), a Polish canon, physician, jurist, and astronomer, was the first to propose a complete and mathematically coherent heliocentric model in his work *De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium* (On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres), published in 1543, the year of his death. Aware of the potentially controversial nature of his ideas, Copernicus had delayed publication for decades.

Copernicus's main propositions, which constituted a radical paradigm shift, were as follows:
1.  **The center of the Universe is not the Earth, but the Sun.** Copernicus placed the Sun, not the Earth, at the center of the planetary system, although he did not consider it the exact center of the entire Universe.
2.  **The Earth rotates on its own axis in 24 hours.** This rotational movement explained the apparent diurnal motion of the stars and the Sun, eliminating the need to make all celestial spheres rotate at a dizzying speed.
3.  **The Earth, like the other planets, revolves around the Sun.** This revolutionary movement explained the annual cycle of seasons and the changing position of constellations.
4.  **The retrograde motion of planets is an optical illusion.** It is due to the Earth's own motion, which overtakes the outer planets or is overtaken by the inner planets in their course around the Sun. This was one of the most elegant simplifications of the Copernican model, eliminating the need for the major epicycles of the Ptolemaic system.

Copernicus's model significantly simplified the description of planetary movements compared to the Ptolemaic system, offering superior conceptual elegance. However, Copernicus, out of respect for Greek tradition and geometric perfection, maintained perfect circular orbits. To explain the observed variations in planetary speeds and distances, he was forced to retain minor epicycles and eccentrics, making his model, in terms of immediate predictive accuracy, not necessarily superior to Ptolemy's at the time. The main objection to his model was the absence of observable stellar parallax: if the Earth revolved around the Sun, nearby stars should show a slight apparent shift over the course of the year. Copernicus explained this by postulating that the stars were at immense distances, far beyond what could be measured.

The Sun is no longer a mere point of light among others, but the "center" and "lamp" of the Universe, a source of light and, implicitly, of order and life. This vision, although motivated by aesthetic and philosophical considerations (the beauty and simplicity of the system) as much as by calculations, paved the way for a deeper understanding of celestial mechanics and decentered humanity from its privileged position in the cosmos.

### 3.2. Tycho Brahe: The Tireless Observer and Compiler of Precise Data

<RealPerson name="Tycho_Brahe" lang="fr" bio="Astronome danois, célèbre pour ses observations astronomiques extrêmement précises réalisées sans télescope. Il a construit l'observatoire d'Uraniborg et a amassé des données qui ont permis à Kepler de formuler ses lois du mouvement planétaire.">Tycho Brahe</RealPerson> (1546-1601) was a Danish astronomer whose major contribution lies in the collection of astronomical data of unparalleled precision for his time, and this, before the invention of the telescope. Convinced of the need for systematic and continuous observations to solve the problems of astronomy, he built, with the support of King Frederick II of Denmark, a sophisticated observatory, Uraniborg, on the island of <Location name="Hven" lang="fr" description="Petite île danoise située dans l'Øresund, entre la Suède et le Danemark. C'est là que l'astronome Tycho Brahe a construit son célèbre observatoire d'Uraniborg.">Hven</Location>.

From 1576 to 1597, Tycho and his team spent decades measuring the positions of stars and planets with an accuracy of a few arcminutes (one arcminute is 1/60th of a degree), an unprecedented precision for naked-eye instruments. He designed and built giant, stable instruments, such as mural quadrants and armillary spheres, to minimize observational errors.
Tycho was not entirely convinced by the Copernican model, mainly due to the absence of observable stellar parallax. He believed that if the Earth revolved around the Sun, stars should appear at slightly different angles at different times of the year. Since he could detect no displacement, he concluded that the Earth must be stationary. He proposed his own model, the Tychonic system, an ingenious compromise where the Earth remained at the center of the Universe, the Sun revolved around the Earth, and all other planets revolved around the Sun. This model explained planetary observations almost as well as the Copernican model without requiring a moving Earth.
However, Tycho's observations were crucial for the astronomical revolution. His discovery of a supernova in 1572 (which he described in *De Nova Stella*) and his study of a comet in 1577 demonstrated that the superlunary heavens were not immutable and perfect as Aristotle had claimed, but subject to change, thus undermining a fundamental pillar of Aristotelian cosmology. The richness and precision of his data would prove invaluable to his successor.

### 3.3. Kepler and the Laws of Planetary Motion: The End of Perfect Circles

<RealPerson name="Johannes_Kepler" lang="fr" bio="Astronome, mathématicien et physicien allemand, célèbre pour avoir découvert les trois lois du mouvement planétaire qui portent son nom. Il a travaillé avec Tycho Brahe et a utilisé ses données pour formuler ces lois, qui ont fourni une base solide au modèle héliocentrique de Copernic.">Johannes Kepler</RealPerson> (1571-1630), a deeply mystical and Platonic German mathematician, was Tycho Brahe's assistant in Prague. After Tycho's death in 1601, Kepler inherited his valuable observational data, particularly concerning the planet Mars, whose orbit was the most difficult to reconcile with circular models. Obsessed with finding mathematical harmony in the Universe and discovering the "laws" that governed celestial movements, Kepler spent years analyzing this data with unprecedented rigor.

His work led to the formulation of his three laws of planetary motion, published between 1609 and 1619, which not only confirmed the heliocentric model but radically perfected it:
1.  **First Law (Law of Orbits):** Published in 1609 in *Astronomia Nova*, this law states that planets describe elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus. This was a bold break with the millennia-old tradition of perfect circular orbits, an idea that even Copernicus had not dared to abandon. This law eliminated the need for epicycles to explain variations in planetary distances.
2.  **Second Law (Law of Areas):** Also published in 1609, it states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. This means that the planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun (at perihelion) and slower when it is farther away (at aphelion). This law described the variation in planetary speed quantitatively and predictively.
3.  **Third Law (Law of Periods):** Published in 1619 in *Harmonices Mundi*, this law states that the square of the orbital period of a planet (T, the time it takes to complete one orbit around the Sun) is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit (a, half of the longest diameter of the ellipse). Mathematically, this is expressed as $$ T^2 \propto a^3 $$. This law revealed a universal mathematical relationship between the movements of all planets in the solar system, suggesting an underlying common physical cause.

Kepler's laws not only confirmed the heliocentric model but also perfected it by replacing circles with ellipses, thus eliminating the need for epicycles and equants. They provided a precise and elegant mathematical description of planetary motion, based on rigorous empirical data, paving the way for a dynamic understanding of the Universe and preparing the ground for Newton's theory of universal gravitation.

### 3.4. Galileo and the Telescope: The Era of Direct Observation and Empirical Proof

<RealPerson name="Galileo_Galilei" lang="fr" bio="Astronome, physicien et ingénieur italien, souvent appelé le 'père de la science observationnelle moderne'. Il a amélioré le télescope et a fait des observations révolutionnaires qui ont soutenu le modèle héliocentrique, notamment les phases de Vénus et les lunes de Jupiter.">Galileo</RealPerson> (1564-1642), an Italian astronomer, physicist, and engineer, was an emblematic figure of the scientific revolution. In 1609, having heard of the invention of the spyglass in the Netherlands, he built an improved version, capable of magnifying up to 30 times, and pointed it at the sky. His telescopic observations were revolutionary and provided direct empirical evidence in favor of the heliocentric model, openly challenging Aristotelian and Ptolemaic dogmas.

His major discoveries, published in his *Sidereus Nuncius* (Starry Messenger) in 1610, include:
*   **Craters and mountains on the Moon:** By observing the Moon, Galileo discovered that it was not a perfect, smooth sphere, as Aristotelian cosmology claimed for celestial bodies, but that it possessed mountains, valleys, and craters, similar to Earth. This challenged the fundamental distinction between an imperfect sublunary world and a perfect superlunary world.
*   **The phases of Venus:** Galileo observed that Venus exhibited phases (crescent, quarter, full) similar to those of the Moon. In the Ptolemaic model, Venus, always being between the Earth and the Sun, should only show a crescent or partial phase. The full phases of Venus, with variations in size, were only explainable if Venus orbited the Sun and not the Earth, as in the heliocentric model. This was irrefutable visual proof against geocentrism.
*   **The four largest moons of Jupiter:** Galileo discovered four satellites orbiting Jupiter (today called Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto, the Galilean moons). This was direct proof that there were celestial bodies that did not orbit the Earth, challenging the principle that everything must orbit our planet. Jupiter and its moons formed a "mini-solar system" in themselves.
*   **Sunspots:** By observing the Sun (with rudimentary precautions), Galileo discovered dark spots on its surface. These spots moved, indicating that the Sun was not immaculate and perfect, but that it rotated on its axis and was subject to changes, again contradicting the perfection and unalterability of Aristotelian celestial bodies.
*   **The Milky Way:** He observed that the Milky Way, that milky band in the sky, was composed of countless individual stars, too faint to be seen with the naked eye. This revealed a vastness of the Universe far greater than had been imagined.

Galileo's observations, published and widely disseminated, were a fatal blow to the geocentric model and Aristotelian dogmas. They provided the tangible empirical evidence that Copernicus's model lacked to convince the most skeptical minds.

Galileo's condemnation by the Roman Inquisition in 1633 is one of the most famous and tragic episodes in the history of science, symbolizing the conflict between new science and religious authority. Galileo, a devout Catholic, sincerely believed that his telescopic discoveries proved the truth of the Copernican model, which he considered compatible with Christian faith, arguing that the Bible spoke the language of men and not that of science. However, the Catholic Church, at the time, firmly adhered to a literal interpretation of Scripture that placed the Earth at the center of the Universe (for example, Joshua 10:12-13 where the Sun stands still), and to the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic model that stemmed from it, integrated into scholastic theology.

As early as 1616, the Inquisition had declared heliocentrism "formally heretical" and had banned Copernicus's work. Galileo was then warned not to "hold or defend" this doctrine. However, the publication of his *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632), where he eloquently defended heliocentrism and featured a geocentric character (Simplicio) who appeared foolish, was perceived as a direct provocation. Accused of heresy and disobedience, Galileo was tried, forced to publicly recant his heliocentric ideas, and spent the rest of his life under house arrest.

This controversy highlights the historical tension between science, which progresses through observation, questioning of dogmas, and empirical verification, and religious or political institutions that may cling to established truths, often for reasons of social stability or power. It underscores the crucial importance of freedom of thought and the scientific method for the advancement of knowledge, even in the face of opposition from authorities. The Galileo affair has become a symbol of science's resistance to obscurantism, although historians have shown that the context was more complex, involving power struggles within the Church and tactical errors on Galileo's part. The Catholic Church officially rehabilitated Galileo only in 1992, acknowledging its error.

### 4.1. Isaac Newton and Universal Gravitation: The Unification of the Cosmos

In his major work, *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy), published in 1687, Newton formulated the universal law of gravitation, one of the greatest intellectual syntheses in history. This law states that two bodies attract each other with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

$$ F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

Where:
*   $F$ is the gravitational force between the two bodies.
*   $G$ is the universal gravitational constant, a constant of proportionality that determines the strength of the interaction.
*   $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two bodies.
*   $r$ is the distance between the centers of mass of the two bodies.

The implications of Newton's law were profound and revolutionary:
*   **Unification of terrestrial and celestial laws:** The law of gravitation explained both the fall of an apple on Earth and the movement of planets around the Sun with one and the same physical law. Aristotle's sublunary and superlunary worlds, with their distinct laws, were definitively abolished. The Universe was now perceived as a coherent whole, governed by universal principles.
*   **Explanation of Kepler's laws:** Newton mathematically demonstrated that Kepler's three laws naturally derived from his law of gravitation and his own laws of motion (inertia, force-acceleration relationship, action-reaction). Elliptical orbits were no longer a mere empirical description, but a necessary consequence of the forces at play. He showed that the force that keeps planets in their orbits is the same as the one that makes objects fall on Earth.
*   **Predictions and verifications:** Newton's theory allowed for highly precise predictions of the movements of celestial bodies, including comets (like Halley's Comet), tides (explained by the gravitational attraction of the Moon and Sun), and perturbations of planetary orbits. It also provided a framework for calculating the masses of planets and the Sun.
*   **Development of calculus:** To formulate and solve the problems posed by his theory of gravitation and his laws of motion, Newton had to develop new mathematical tools, notably differential and integral calculus, which he presented in the *Principia*.

Newton's universal gravitation marked the culmination of the scientific revolution and established a new paradigm for physics and astronomy, which would dominate for over two centuries. It transformed cosmology from a descriptive science into an explanatory and predictive science, based on fundamental physical principles.

### 4.2. First Estimates of the Scale of the Universe: Pushing the Boundaries of the Known

With a better understanding of celestial mechanics and more powerful mathematical tools, astronomers began to tackle the fundamental question of the Universe's scale. Until then, distances were largely unknown or underestimated.

*   **Measurement of the Earth-Sun Distance (Astronomical Unit):** Precise knowledge of the distance between the Earth and the Sun (the astronomical unit, AU) was crucial for determining the scale of the solar system. In 1672, <RealPerson name="Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" lang="fr" bio="Astronome, ingénieur et mathématicien italien, naturalisé français. Il est célèbre pour ses observations des planètes, notamment la découverte de la division de Cassini dans les anneaux de Saturne et la mesure de la distance Terre-Soleil.">Giovanni Domenico Cassini</RealPerson>, director of the Paris Observatory, and his collaborator <RealPerson name="Jean_Richer" lang="fr" bio="Astronome français, connu pour ses travaux sur la parallaxe de Mars et la détermination de la distance Terre-Soleil en collaboration avec Cassini.">Jean Richer</RealPerson> used the parallax method to estimate this distance. Richer observed Mars from Cayenne (French Guiana), while Cassini simultaneously observed it from Paris. By measuring the slight apparent shift of Mars relative to background stars from two distant points on Earth, they were able to triangulate its position and deduce the Earth-Mars distance, and then, using Kepler's laws, the Earth-Sun distance. They obtained a value of approximately 140 million kilometers, remarkably close to the modern value of 149.6 million kilometers. This measurement allowed for the first time to set a concrete scale for the solar system.

*   **Stellar Parallax and the Vastness of Interstellar Space:** The idea that stars should show a slight apparent shift (annual parallax) if the Earth revolved around the Sun was a key prediction of the heliocentric model. However, despite the efforts of many astronomers, this shift was imperceptible with the instruments of the time, which had been a major argument against Copernicus. This implied that stars were at unimaginably great distances. It was not until 1838, more than two centuries after Copernicus, that <RealPerson name="Friedrich_Bessel" lang="fr" bio="Astronome, mathématicien et géodésien allemand. Il est célèbre pour avoir été le premier à mesurer la parallaxe d'une étoile (61 Cygni) en 1838, prouvant ainsi le mouvement de la Terre et la vastitude de l'espace interstellaire.">Friedrich Bessel</RealPerson>, a German astronomer, succeeded in measuring the parallax of the star 61 Cygni. He found a tiny displacement of 0.31 arcseconds, which corresponded to a distance of approximately 10.4 light-years. This measurement was irrefutable proof of the Earth's motion and revealed the immense distance of the stars, confirming that the Universe was far vaster than anyone had imagined. Other astronomers, such as Thomas Henderson and Wilhelm Struve, also succeeded in measuring stellar parallaxes shortly after Bessel, confirming his results.

These measurements began to reveal the true immensity of the Universe, far beyond the celestial spheres imagined by the Greeks. The question of whether the Universe was infinite or finite, and whether our Sun was one star among many, began to arise with new urgency, paving the way for the galactic and extragalactic astronomy of subsequent centuries. Understanding the scale of the Universe is an ongoing quest, with each new measurement pushing the boundaries of our knowledge and reminding us of our modest place in the cosmos.

Humanity's journey to understand the Universe is a fascinating intellectual odyssey, marked by myths, philosophical speculations, and, ultimately, rigorous observations and mathematical theories. From primitive cosmogonies, where gods shaped the world and man was at the center of everything, to Ptolemy's complex geocentric models that attempted to reconcile observations and dogmas, man has always sought to make sense of his cosmic environment, to find order and meaning within it. These early attempts, though not scientific in the modern sense, posed fundamental questions about the origin, structure, and destiny of the cosmos.

The heliocentric revolution, initiated by Copernicus's audacious propositions and decisively supported by Galileo's revolutionary telescopic observations and Kepler's mathematically precise laws, marked a decisive turning point in the history of thought. It decentered the Earth and, by extension, humanity, from the privileged position they occupied at the heart of the Universe. This "decentering" was not merely an astronomical correction; it had profound implications for philosophy, theology, and the perception of humanity's place in the cosmos. Isaac Newton's work, with his law of universal gravitation, consolidated this new understanding by unifying terrestrial and celestial laws, demonstrating that the same physical principles govern the entire Universe, from the fall of an apple to the orbit of planets.

These foundational steps not only transformed our understanding of space and motion; they also established the pillars of the modern scientific method, based on systematic observation, experimentation (even if limited in astronomy), mathematical modeling, the formulation of testable hypotheses, and the constant questioning of existing paradigms. The transition from myths to the first scientific observations was a slow, often conflictual and arduous process, but it laid the groundwork for contemporary astrophysics, a discipline that continues to explore the mysteries of the Universe with ever more sophisticated tools and theories, from space telescopes to particle accelerators.

Our journey through the history of cosmological thought reminds us that science is a dynamic human endeavor, constantly evolving, where each new discovery opens the door to new questions and an ever deeper understanding of our place in the immense and wonderful cosmic tapestry. It is an endless quest, driven by curiosity and the insatiable desire of the human spirit to unravel the secrets of the Universe.

### Glossary

*   **Cosmogony**: A narrative or theory explaining the origin and formation of the Universe.
*   **Geocentrism**: A cosmological model placing the Earth motionless at the center of the Universe, around which all other celestial bodies revolve.
*   **Heliocentrism**: A cosmological model placing the Sun at the center of the planetary system, around which the Earth and other planets revolve.
*   **Epicycle**: A small circle on which a celestial body is supposed to move, whose center itself revolves on a larger circle (the deferent) around the Earth, used in the Ptolemaic model to explain retrograde motions.
*   **Stellar parallax**: The apparent displacement of a star relative to more distant stars, due to the Earth's orbital motion around the Sun, used to measure stellar distances.
*   **Astronomical Unit (AU)**: A unit of distance equal to the average distance between the Earth and the Sun, approximately 149.6 million kilometers.
