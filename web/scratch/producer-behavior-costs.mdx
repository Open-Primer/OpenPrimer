## Introduction: The Logic of the Firm

Welcome to this foundational lesson in Introductory Microeconomics, where we embark on a journey to understand the intricate decisions made by producers. Just as consumers strive to maximize their utility given their budget constraints, firms, as the primary agents of production, aim to maximize their profits. This pursuit of profit maximization drives their choices regarding what to produce, how much to produce, and how to combine inputs.

Understanding producer behavior is crucial for comprehending how markets function, how prices are determined, and how resources are allocated within an economy. We will construct a robust model of the firm, starting from basic assumptions and progressively building up to complex decision-making processes. Our exploration will involve the interplay of production capabilities, the costs associated with these capabilities, and ultimately, the firm's supply decisions in various market structures. This lesson will equip you with the analytical tools to dissect and interpret the economic rationale behind business operations.

### Learning Objectives

Upon successful completion of this lesson, you will be able to:

**Knowledge (Savoir):**
*   **Define** and differentiate between key concepts such as production function, short-run vs. long-run, various cost categories (fixed, variable, marginal, average), and economies of scale.
*   **Explain** the Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns and its implications for production and costs.
*   **Identify** the conditions for profit maximization for a firm in a competitive market.

**Skills (Savoir-faire):**
*   **Analyze** and interpret production and cost curves graphically and algebraically.
*   **Derive** a firm's short-run supply curve from its marginal cost curve.
*   **Apply** the profit maximization rule ($MR=MC$) to determine optimal output levels.

**Attitudes (Posture/Analyse):**
*   **Critically evaluate** the assumptions underlying the standard model of producer behavior and recognize its limitations.
*   **Appreciate** the interconnectedness of production, cost, and supply decisions in shaping market outcomes.
*   **Develop** a systematic approach to analyzing economic problems from the perspective of a firm.

## 1. Production Theory: Foundations of Output

At the heart of producer behavior lies the process of production – the transformation of inputs into outputs. A firm exists to organize this process, combining various resources to create goods and services that consumers demand.

### 1.1 The Firm and the Production Function

A <Glossary term="Firm" definition="An economic unit that hires factors of production and organizes them to produce and sell goods and services.">firm</Glossary> is an organization that transforms inputs (factors of production) into outputs (goods and services). The fundamental relationship between the quantity of inputs used and the maximum quantity of output that can be produced is described by the <Glossary term="Production Function" definition="A mathematical or graphical representation showing the maximum quantity of output that can be produced from any given combination of inputs, given the existing technology.">production function</Glossary>.

Mathematically, a production function can be expressed as:
$$ Q = f(L, K, M, \dots) $$
Where:
*   $Q$ represents the quantity of output.
*   $L$ represents labor (e.g., number of workers, hours worked).
*   $K$ represents capital (e.g., machinery, buildings).
*   $M$ represents raw materials.
*   The ellipsis ($\dots$) indicates that other inputs like land, energy, or technology could also be included.

> [!NOTE]
> The production function assumes a given level of technology. Any technological advancement would shift the production function, allowing more output to be produced with the same inputs, or the same output with fewer inputs.

### 1.2 Short Run vs. Long Run in Production

The distinction between the short run and the long run is crucial in production theory, not because of a specific calendar period, but due to the flexibility of inputs.

*   **<Glossary term="Short Run (Production)" definition="A period of time in which at least one factor of production is fixed in quantity. Typically, capital is considered fixed, while labor is variable.">Short Run</Glossary>**: In the short run, at least one factor of production is fixed. Typically, capital (e.g., factory size, number of machines) is considered fixed, while labor and raw materials are variable. Firms can adjust output by changing the amount of variable inputs.
*   **<Glossary term="Long Run (Production)" definition="A period of time in which all factors of production are variable. Firms can adjust the scale of their operations, including changing capital stock.">Long Run</Glossary>**: In the long run, all factors of production are variable. Firms have enough time to adjust their capital stock, build new factories, or exit an industry. There are no fixed inputs in the long run.

For simplicity, we often analyze the short run by assuming capital ($K$) is fixed and labor ($L$) is variable. Thus, the short-run production function becomes $Q = f(L, \bar{K})$, where $\bar{K}$ denotes fixed capital.

### 1.3 Total Product, Marginal Product, and Average Product

To understand how output changes as we vary a single input (e.g., labor) in the short run, we use three key measures:

1.  **<Glossary term="Total Product (TP)" definition="The total quantity of output produced by a firm for a given quantity of inputs.">Total Product (TP)</Glossary>**: The total quantity of output produced by a firm for a given quantity of inputs. As more labor is added to a fixed amount of capital, total product generally increases, initially at an increasing rate, then at a decreasing rate, and eventually may decline.

2.  **<Glossary term="Marginal Product (MP)" definition="The additional output produced by adding one more unit of a variable input (e.g., labor), while holding all other inputs constant.">Marginal Product (MP)</Glossary>**: The additional output produced by adding one more unit of a variable input (e.g., labor), while holding all other inputs constant.
    $$ MP_L = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta L} $$
    Where $\Delta Q$ is the change in total output and $\Delta L$ is the change in labor input.

3.  **<Glossary term="Average Product (AP)" definition="The total output produced per unit of a variable input (e.g., labor). It is calculated as total product divided by the quantity of the variable input.">Average Product (AP)</Glossary>**: The total output produced per unit of a variable input.
    $$ AP_L = \frac{Q}{L} $$

#### 1.3.1 The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

The relationship between MP and AP is critical and is governed by the <Glossary term="Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns" definition="A principle stating that as successive units of a variable input are added to a fixed input, beyond some point, the marginal product of the variable input will eventually decrease.">Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns</Glossary>. This law states that as successive units of a variable input (e.g., labor) are added to a fixed input (e.g., capital), beyond some point, the marginal product of the variable input will eventually decrease.

> [!NOTE]
> This law is a short-run phenomenon. It occurs because the fixed input becomes increasingly congested or overutilized as more of the variable input is added, leading to less efficient production.

**Graphical Representation:**

Imagine a graph with labor on the x-axis and output on the y-axis.

*   **Total Product (TP) Curve**: Starts at the origin, rises, initially at an increasing rate (convex), then at a decreasing rate (concave), reaches a maximum, and then may decline.
*   **Marginal Product (MP) Curve**: Initially rises, reaches a maximum (at the inflection point of the TP curve), then declines, and eventually can become negative.
*   **Average Product (AP) Curve**: Rises, reaches a maximum, and then declines. The MP curve intersects the AP curve at the AP curve's maximum point.

## 2. Cost Theory: The Price of Production

Production is not free; it incurs costs. Understanding a firm's cost structure is paramount to analyzing its supply decisions and profitability. Costs are directly linked to the production function and the prices of inputs.

### 2.1 Explicit vs. Implicit Costs and Opportunity Cost

Economists distinguish between different types of costs:

*   **<Glossary term="Explicit Costs" definition="Direct, out-of-pocket payments for inputs, such as wages, rent, and raw materials. These are typically recorded by accountants.">Explicit Costs</Glossary>**: These are direct, out-of-pocket payments for inputs, such as wages for labor, rent for land, and payments for raw materials. These are the costs that accountants typically record.
*   **<Glossary term="Implicit Costs" definition="The opportunity costs of using resources owned by the firm or provided by the firm's owners. They do not involve a direct money payment.">Implicit Costs</Glossary>**: These are the opportunity costs of using resources owned by the firm or provided by the firm's owners. They do not involve a direct money payment. For example, the implicit cost of capital is the foregone interest that could have been earned if the money invested in the business had been invested elsewhere. The implicit cost of the owner's time is the salary they could have earned working for someone else.
*   **<Glossary term="Opportunity Cost" definition="The value of the next best alternative that must be foregone when a choice is made. In economics, all costs are ultimately opportunity costs.">Opportunity Cost</Glossary>**: The value of the next best alternative that must be foregone when a choice is made. In economics, all costs are ultimately opportunity costs. Economic profit considers both explicit and implicit costs, while accounting profit only considers explicit costs.

$$ \text{Economic Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - (\text{Explicit Costs} + \text{Implicit Costs}) $$
$$ \text{Accounting Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Explicit Costs} $$

> [!NOTE]
> A firm earning zero economic profit is still doing well, as it is covering all its explicit costs and earning a normal rate of return on its capital and labor (i.e., covering its implicit costs). This is often referred to as a <Glossary term="Normal Profit" definition="The minimum level of profit needed for a company to remain competitive in the market. It is the profit that covers all explicit and implicit costs, including the opportunity cost of the owner's capital and time.">normal profit</Glossary>.

### 2.2 Short-Run Costs

In the short run, with at least one fixed input, costs can be categorized as fixed or variable.

*   **<Glossary term="Fixed Costs (FC)" definition="Costs that do not vary with the level of output in the short run. Examples include rent, insurance, and depreciation of capital.">Fixed Costs (FC)</Glossary>**: Costs that do not vary with the level of output. These costs are incurred even if no output is produced. Examples include rent on a factory, insurance premiums, and salaries of administrative staff that are not directly tied to production volume.
*   **<Glossary term="Variable Costs (VC)" definition="Costs that change with the level of output. Examples include wages for production workers, raw materials, and energy costs.">Variable Costs (VC)</Glossary>**: Costs that change with the level of output. These costs are zero if no output is produced and increase as output increases. Examples include wages for production workers, raw materials, and electricity used in production.
*   **<Glossary term="Total Cost (TC)" definition="The sum of fixed costs and variable costs at each level of output.">Total Cost (TC)</Glossary>**: The sum of fixed costs and variable costs.
    $$ TC = FC + VC $$

From these total costs, we can derive average and marginal costs:

*   **<Glossary term="Average Fixed Cost (AFC)" definition="Total fixed cost divided by the quantity of output. AFC declines continuously as output increases.">Average Fixed Cost (AFC)</Glossary>**: Fixed cost per unit of output.
    $$ AFC = \frac{FC}{Q} $$
    AFC continuously declines as output increases, a phenomenon known as "spreading the overhead."
*   **<Glossary term="Average Variable Cost (AVC)" definition="Total variable cost divided by the quantity of output. AVC typically falls initially and then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.">Average Variable Cost (AVC)</Glossary>**: Variable cost per unit of output.
    $$ AVC = \frac{VC}{Q} $$
    AVC typically falls initially as production becomes more efficient, then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.
*   **<Glossary term="Average Total Cost (ATC)" definition="Total cost divided by the quantity of output, or the sum of AFC and AVC. ATC is typically U-shaped.">Average Total Cost (ATC)</Glossary>**: Total cost per unit of output.
    $$ ATC = \frac{TC}{Q} = AFC + AVC $$
    ATC is also typically U-shaped, reflecting the combined effects of declining AFC and rising AVC.
*   **<Glossary term="Marginal Cost (MC)" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">Marginal Cost (MC)</Glossary>**: The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output.
    $$ MC = \frac{\Delta TC}{\Delta Q} = \frac{\Delta VC}{\Delta Q} $$
    MC is crucial for decision-making as it tells the firm the cost of producing one more unit. The MC curve typically falls initially and then rises sharply, reflecting the law of diminishing marginal returns.

#### 2.2.1 Relationship Between Production and Cost Curves

There's an inverse relationship between the product curves (MP, AP) and the variable cost curves (MC, AVC).

*   When <Glossary term="Marginal Product (MP)" definition="The additional output produced by adding one more unit of a variable input (e.g., labor), while holding all other inputs constant.">MP</Glossary> is rising, <Glossary term="Marginal Cost (MC)" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">MC</Glossary> is falling.
*   When <Glossary term="MP" definition="The additional output produced by adding one more unit of a variable input (e.g., labor), while holding all other inputs constant.">MP</Glossary> is falling, <Glossary term="MC" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">MC</Glossary> is rising.
*   When <Glossary term="Average Product (AP)" definition="The total output produced per unit of a variable input (e.g., labor). It is calculated as total product divided by the quantity of the variable input.">AP</Glossary> is rising, <Glossary term="Average Variable Cost (AVC)" definition="Total variable cost divided by the quantity of output. AVC typically falls initially and then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.">AVC</Glossary> is falling.
*   When <Glossary term="AP" definition="The total output produced per unit of a variable input (e.g., labor). It is calculated as total product divided by the quantity of the variable input.">AP</Glossary> is falling, <Glossary term="AVC" definition="Total variable cost divided by the quantity of output. AVC typically falls initially and then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.">AVC</Glossary> is rising.
*   <Glossary term="MC" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">MC</Glossary> intersects both <Glossary term="AVC" definition="Total variable cost divided by the quantity of output. AVC typically falls initially and then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.">AVC</Glossary> and <Glossary term="Average Total Cost (ATC)" definition="Total cost divided by the quantity of output, or the sum of AFC and AVC. ATC is typically U-shaped.">ATC</Glossary> at their minimum points.

### 2.3 Long-Run Costs: Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

In the long run, all inputs are variable, allowing firms to choose the optimal scale of operation. The <Glossary term="Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve" definition="A curve that shows the lowest average cost at which a firm can produce any given level of output when all inputs are variable. It is the envelope of all short-run average total cost curves.">Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve</Glossary> shows the lowest average cost at which a firm can produce any given level of output when all inputs are variable. It is often depicted as an envelope of many short-run ATC curves.

The shape of the LRAC curve reveals important concepts:

*   **<Glossary term="Economies of Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production decreases as the scale of output increases. This can be due to specialization, bulk purchasing, or efficient use of large-scale machinery.">Economies of Scale</Glossary>**: Occur when the LRAC falls as output increases. This can be due to:
    *   Specialization of labor and machinery.
    *   Bulk purchasing discounts for inputs.
    *   More efficient use of large-scale machinery.
    *   Better organization and management.
*   **<Glossary term="Diseconomies of Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production increases as the scale of output increases. This can be due to coordination problems, communication breakdowns, or bureaucratic inefficiencies in very large organizations.">Diseconomies of Scale</Glossary>**: Occur when the LRAC rises as output increases. This can be due to:
    *   Coordination and communication problems in very large organizations.
    *   Bureaucratic inefficiencies.
    *   Difficulty in motivating a large workforce.
*   **<Glossary term="Constant Returns to Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production remains constant as the scale of output increases. This occurs when a proportional increase in all inputs leads to an equally proportional increase in output.">Constant Returns to Scale</Glossary>**: Occur when the LRAC remains constant as output increases.

## 3. Profit Maximization: The Firm's Objective

The primary objective of most firms in mainstream economic theory is to maximize economic profit. To achieve this, firms must make decisions about how much output to produce.

### 3.1 Total Revenue, Total Cost, and Total Profit

*   **<Glossary term="Total Revenue (TR)" definition="The total amount of money a firm receives from selling its output. It is calculated as price per unit multiplied by the quantity sold (TR = P × Q).">Total Revenue (TR)</Glossary>**: The total amount of money a firm receives from selling its output.
    $$ TR = P \times Q $$
    Where $P$ is the price per unit and $Q$ is the quantity sold.
*   **<Glossary term="Total Cost (TC)" definition="The sum of fixed costs and variable costs at each level of output.">Total Cost (TC)</Glossary>**: As discussed, the sum of fixed and variable costs.
*   **<Glossary term="Total Profit ($\pi$)" definition="The difference between total revenue and total cost. Economic profit considers both explicit and implicit costs.">Total Profit ($\pi$)</Glossary>**: The difference between total revenue and total cost.
    $$ \pi = TR - TC $$

A firm maximizes profit by choosing the output level where the difference between TR and TC is greatest.

### 3.2 Marginal Revenue and Marginal Cost: The Profit-Maximizing Rule

While looking at total profit is useful, firms make decisions at the margin.

*   **<Glossary term="Marginal Revenue (MR)" definition="The additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of output.">Marginal Revenue (MR)</Glossary>**: The additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of output.
    $$ MR = \frac{\Delta TR}{\Delta Q} $$
*   **<Glossary term="Marginal Cost (MC)" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">Marginal Cost (MC)</Glossary>**: The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output.

The fundamental rule for profit maximization is to produce at the output level where <Glossary term="Marginal Revenue (MR)" definition="The additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of output.">Marginal Revenue (MR)</Glossary> equals <Glossary term="Marginal Cost (MC)" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">Marginal Cost (MC)</Glossary>.

**The MR = MC Rule:**
*   If $MR > MC$: Producing an additional unit adds more to revenue than to cost, so profit increases. The firm should increase output.
*   If $MR < MC$: Producing an additional unit adds more to cost than to revenue, so profit decreases. The firm should decrease output.
*   If $MR = MC$: The firm is producing the profit-maximizing output level. Any deviation from this level would reduce profit.

#### 3.2.1 Profit Maximization in Perfect Competition

In a <Glossary term="Perfect Competition" definition="A market structure characterized by a large number of buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, free entry and exit, and price-taking behavior by individual firms.">perfectly competitive market</Glossary>, individual firms are <Glossary term="Price Taker" definition="A firm or consumer that cannot affect the market price and must accept the prevailing market price for its goods, services, or resources.">price takers</Glossary>. This means they cannot influence the market price and must sell their output at the going market price. For a price-taking firm:
$$ P = MR $$
Therefore, in perfect competition, the profit-maximizing rule simplifies to:
$$ P = MC $$

<Epistemology title="Beyond Profit Maximization: Alternative Theories of the Firm">
While the profit maximization model is a cornerstone of neoclassical economics, it has faced considerable criticism and alternative theories have emerged. <HistoricalPerson name="Herbert_A._Simon" lang="en">Herbert A. Simon</HistoricalPerson>, a Nobel laureate, introduced the concept of <Glossary term="Satisficing" definition="A decision-making strategy that aims for a satisfactory or 'good enough' result, rather than the optimal solution. Firms might satisfice due to incomplete information, cognitive limitations, or other objectives besides pure profit maximization.">satisficing</Glossary>, suggesting that firms, due to incomplete information and cognitive limitations, may aim for 'good enough' profits rather than truly maximizing them. Other theories propose that firms might prioritize sales maximization (e.g., to gain market share), managerial utility maximization (where managers pursue their own interests), or long-run survival. These alternative perspectives highlight the complexity of real-world business decisions and the limitations of a single-objective model, especially in large, complex organizations where ownership and management are separated.
</Epistemology>

## 4. Supply: The Firm's Output Decision

The profit-maximization rule ($P=MC$ for competitive firms) directly leads to the derivation of a firm's supply curve.

### 4.1 The Firm's Short-Run Supply Curve

For a perfectly competitive firm, its <Glossary term="Short-Run Supply Curve" definition="The portion of a firm's marginal cost curve that lies above its average variable cost curve. It shows the quantity of output the firm will supply at different prices in the short run.">short-run supply curve</Glossary> is its marginal cost (MC) curve above the minimum point of its average variable cost (AVC) curve.

**Why?**
1.  **Profit Maximization**: The firm produces where $P = MC$. So, for any given price, the MC curve tells us the quantity the firm will supply.
2.  **Shutdown Rule**: A firm will continue to produce in the short run as long as $P \ge AVC$. If $P < AVC$, the firm cannot even cover its variable costs, so it minimizes losses by shutting down (producing zero output). Therefore, the portion of the MC curve below the minimum AVC is not part of the supply curve.

### 4.2 Factors Affecting Supply

Changes in factors other than the price of the good itself will shift the entire supply curve. These include:

*   **Input Prices**: An increase in the price of inputs (e.g., wages, raw materials) increases production costs, shifting the MC and AVC curves upward, and thus shifting the supply curve to the left (decrease in supply).
*   **Technology**: Technological advancements reduce production costs, shifting the MC and AVC curves downward, and thus shifting the supply curve to the right (increase in supply).
*   **Expectations**: If firms expect future prices to rise, they might reduce current supply to sell more later, shifting current supply to the left.
*   **Number of Sellers**: An increase in the number of firms in the market will increase the overall market supply, shifting the market supply curve to the right.
*   **Government Policies**: Taxes (decrease supply) or subsidies (increase supply) can shift the supply curve.

### 4.3 Market Supply

The <Glossary term="Market Supply Curve" definition="The horizontal summation of all individual firms' supply curves in a given market. It shows the total quantity of a good or service that all producers are willing and able to supply at different prices.">market supply curve</Glossary> is the horizontal summation of all individual firms' supply curves in a given market. At each price, we sum the quantities supplied by all individual firms to get the total quantity supplied to the market.

### Glossary

<Glossary term="Accounting Profit" definition="The difference between total revenue and explicit costs. It does not account for implicit costs or opportunity costs.">Accounting Profit</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Average Fixed Cost (AFC)" definition="Total fixed cost divided by the quantity of output. AFC declines continuously as output increases.">Average Fixed Cost (AFC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Average Product (AP)" definition="The total output produced per unit of a variable input (e.g., labor). It is calculated as total product divided by the quantity of the variable input.">Average Product (AP)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Average Total Cost (ATC)" definition="Total cost divided by the quantity of output, or the sum of AFC and AVC. ATC is typically U-shaped.">Average Total Cost (ATC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Average Variable Cost (AVC)" definition="Total variable cost divided by the quantity of output. AVC typically falls initially and then rises due to diminishing marginal returns.">Average Variable Cost (AVC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Constant Returns to Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production remains constant as the scale of output increases. This occurs when a proportional increase in all inputs leads to an equally proportional increase in output.">Constant Returns to Scale</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Diseconomies of Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production increases as the scale of output increases. This can be due to coordination problems, communication breakdowns, or bureaucratic inefficiencies in very large organizations.">Diseconomies of Scale</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Economic Profit" definition="The difference between total revenue and total economic costs (explicit costs + implicit costs). It accounts for the opportunity cost of all resources.">Economic Profit</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Economies of Scale" definition="A situation where the long-run average cost of production decreases as the scale of output increases. This can be due to specialization, bulk purchasing, or efficient use of large-scale machinery.">Economies of Scale</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Explicit Costs" definition="Direct, out-of-pocket payments for inputs, such as wages, rent, and raw materials. These are typically recorded by accountants.">Explicit Costs</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Firm" definition="An economic unit that hires factors of production and organizes them to produce and sell goods and services.">Firm</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Fixed Costs (FC)" definition="Costs that do not vary with the level of output in the short run. Examples include rent, insurance, and depreciation of capital.">Fixed Costs (FC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Implicit Costs" definition="The opportunity costs of using resources owned by the firm or provided by the firm's owners. They do not involve a direct money payment.">Implicit Costs</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns" definition="A principle stating that as successive units of a variable input are added to a fixed input, beyond some point, the marginal product of the variable input will eventually decrease.">Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Long Run (Production)" definition="A period of time in which all factors of production are variable. Firms can adjust the scale of their operations, including changing capital stock.">Long Run (Production)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve" definition="A curve that shows the lowest average cost at which a firm can produce any given level of output when all inputs are variable. It is the envelope of all short-run average total cost curves.">Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Marginal Cost (MC)" definition="The additional cost incurred by producing one more unit of output. It is the change in total cost (or total variable cost) divided by the change in output.">Marginal Cost (MC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Marginal Product (MP)" definition="The additional output produced by adding one more unit of a variable input (e.g., labor), while holding all other inputs constant.">Marginal Product (MP)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Marginal Revenue (MR)" definition="The additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of output.">Marginal Revenue (MR)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Market Supply Curve" definition="The horizontal summation of all individual firms' supply curves in a given market. It shows the total quantity of a good or service that all producers are willing and able to supply at different prices.">Market Supply Curve</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)" definition="The lowest point on the long-run average cost curve, representing the smallest output level at which a firm can achieve the lowest possible average cost of production.">Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="MR = MC Rule" definition="The fundamental principle of profit maximization, stating that a firm maximizes its profit by producing the quantity of output where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.">MR = MC Rule</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Normal Profit" definition="The minimum level of profit needed for a company to remain competitive in the market. It is the profit that covers all explicit and implicit costs, including the opportunity cost of the owner's capital and time.">Normal Profit</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Opportunity Cost" definition="The value of the next best alternative that must be foregone when a choice is made. In economics, all costs are ultimately opportunity costs.">Opportunity Cost</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Perfect Competition" definition="A market structure characterized by a large number of buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, free entry and exit, and price-taking behavior by individual firms.">Perfect Competition</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Price Taker" definition="A firm or consumer that cannot affect the market price and must accept the prevailing market price for its goods, services, or resources.">Price Taker</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Production Function" definition="A mathematical or graphical representation showing the maximum quantity of output that can be produced from any given combination of inputs, given the existing technology.">Production Function</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Satisficing" definition="A decision-making strategy that aims for a satisfactory or 'good enough' result, rather than the optimal solution. Firms might satisfice due to incomplete information, cognitive limitations, or other objectives besides pure profit maximization.">Satisficing</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Short Run (Production)" definition="A period of time in which at least one factor of production is fixed in quantity. Typically, capital is considered fixed, while labor is variable.">Short Run (Production)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Short-Run Supply Curve" definition="The portion of a firm's marginal cost curve that lies above its average variable cost curve. It shows the quantity of output the firm will supply at different prices in the short run.">Short-Run Supply Curve</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Total Cost (TC)" definition="The sum of fixed costs and variable costs at each level of output.">Total Cost (TC)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Total Product (TP)" definition="The total quantity of output produced by a firm for a given quantity of inputs.">Total Product (TP)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Total Profit ($\pi$)" definition="The difference between total revenue and total cost. Economic profit considers both explicit and implicit costs.">Total Profit ($\pi$)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Total Revenue (TR)" definition="The total amount of money a firm receives from selling its output. It is calculated as price per unit multiplied by the quantity sold (TR = P × Q).">Total Revenue (TR)</Glossary>
<Glossary term="Variable Costs (VC)" definition="Costs that change with the level of output. Examples include wages for production workers, raw materials, and energy costs.">Variable Costs (VC)</Glossary>
