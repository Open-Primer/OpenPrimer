# Newton's Laws of Motion: The Foundation of Classical Mechanics

## Chapter 1: The Aristotelian Transition & Galileo’s Insight

The classical view of motion, dominated by Aristotelian physics for two millennia, posited that the "natural state" of an object is rest, and that force is required to maintain constant velocity. This is empirically flawed. 

Galileo Galilei dismantled this through the study of inclined planes. He observed that as a ball rolls down an incline and onto a secondary plane, it attempts to return to its original height. If the secondary plane is leveled to the horizontal, the ball theoretically continues to move indefinitely in the absence of external resistive forces.

## Chapter 2: The First Law: Inertia and Reference Frames

Newton’s First Law, the Law of Inertia, states: *An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion remains in motion at a constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force.*

### Mathematical Definition
Let $\mathbf{v}$ be the velocity vector.
$$\sum \mathbf{F} = 0 \implies \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 0 \implies \mathbf{v} = \text{constant}$$

### Inertial vs. Non-Inertial Frames
An inertial frame is one in which the First Law holds true. If an observer is in an accelerating frame (a non-inertial frame), objects appear to move without force, necessitating the introduction of "fictitious forces" (e.g., the Coriolis effect or centrifugal force).

## Chapter 3: The Second Law: The Quantification of Force

The Second Law provides the bridge between geometry and dynamics. It defines how force manifests as a change in the state of motion.

### Derivation
Newton originally defined force as the time rate of change of momentum ($\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$):
$$\mathbf{F} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(m\mathbf{v})$$
Assuming constant mass ($m$):
$$\mathbf{F} = m \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = m\mathbf{a}$$

### Industrial Application: Atwood's Machine
An Atwood machine consists of two masses ($m_1, m_2$) connected by an inextensible string over a massless, frictionless pulley.

**Derivation:**
For mass $m_1$ (assuming $m_2 > m_1$):
1. $T - m_1g = m_1a$
2. $m_2g - T = m_2a$

Adding the equations:
$m_2g - m_1g = (m_1 + m_2)a$
$$a = g \frac{m_2 - m_1}{m_2 + m_1}$$

## Chapter 4: The Third Law: Symmetry in Interaction

The Third Law posits that forces are not isolated occurrences but interactions between two bodies. 

### The Law
For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction:
$$\mathbf{F}_{AB} = -\mathbf{F}_{BA}$$

**Critical Rigor:** These forces act on **different bodies**. Therefore, they do not cancel each other out in a system summation unless the system includes both objects.

## Chapter 5: Synthesis: Free Body Diagrams (FBDs)

Resolution of complex mechanical systems requires the decomposition of all acting forces into orthogonal components.

### Methodology
1. **Isolate the body:** Remove the environment.
2. **Identify all forces:** Gravity ($mg$), Normal Force ($N$), Tension ($T$), Friction ($f$).
3. **Establish a coordinate system:** Align axes to simplify acceleration components.
4. **Apply $\sum \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$.**

### Complex Scenario: Inclined Plane
An object on an incline with angle $\theta$.
*   Normal Force: $N = mg\cos(\theta)$
*   Parallel Force: $F_{||} = mg\sin(\theta)$

If the object is in equilibrium ($\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$), then:
$$f_s = mg\sin(\theta)$$
